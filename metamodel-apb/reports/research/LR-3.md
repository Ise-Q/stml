# Macro / Term-Structure Features with Published Predictive Content for Commodity & Index-Futures Returns — Ranked & PIT-Lagged

**Bottom line:** The strongest, most replicable predictor of *cross-sectional* commodity-futures returns is the **futures basis / roll-yield (carry)**, which proxies the theory-of-storage convenience yield and subsumes inventory information [1][2][3]; for *energy* the dominant event-feature is the **EIA inventory surprise**; for *index/bond* returns the best-evidenced macro features are the **variance risk premium (VRP)**, the **VIX-futures term-structure slope**, and **credit spreads / the excess bond premium**. Every feature below can be made point-in-time (PIT) safe with a documented release lag; the universal safeguard is a **one-day implementation lag** on top of the public-release timestamp, consistent with real-time-data discipline [21].

**TL;DR**
- **Carry/basis is rank #1** and is the single factor that explains the cross-section of commodity "spot" premia [2]; but front-month-only OHLCV *cannot* compute it without a second contract — it must come from `additional_data.xlsx`.
- **EIA inventories, VRP, VIX-slope, credit OAS, gold↔real-rates, copper↔China-PMI, DXY** are all empirically supported and all map onto series already in `additional_data.xlsx`; most are daily and need only a **T+1** lag, except EIA (weekly, ~5–6 day reference lag) and PMI (monthly, released at/after month-end).
- The biggest look-ahead traps are the **reference-vs-release gap** on weekly EIA reports and monthly PMI, and the **3-day Tuesday→Friday reporting lag** on CFTC open-interest/positioning data [22].

## Coverage Against the S1.7 Universe, Confidence Levels & PIT Conventions

**Can be supported directly from `additional_data.xlsx` + front-month OHLCV (CAN):** EIA inventories (WPSR/WNGSR), TIPS/BE10Y, DXY, CHINA_PMI, VIX term-slope, MOVE, and HY/IG OAS are all explicitly provided, so features #2–#8 and #10 are computable. The **VRP** (#3) is computable because VIX is provided and realized variance can be built from the front-month OHLCV history. Time-series momentum and volatility on each contract are computable from front-month OHLCV alone.

**Cannot be computed from front-month-only OHLCV (CANNOT, unless `additional_data` supplies it):** The **basis / roll-yield (carry)** feature (#1) requires a *second* contract (2nd-nearby or a deferred future) to form the futures-price ratio; front-month OHLCV alone cannot produce it. Likewise **aggregate open interest / hedging pressure** (#9, #11) require CFTC/exchange open-interest data not present in price-only OHLCV. These must be sourced into `additional_data.xlsx`; otherwise feature #1 — the single strongest commodity cross-section predictor — is unavailable, and the loader should fall back to time-series momentum on the front contract as an imperfect proxy.

### Confidence levels (strength of published predictive evidence)

- **High:** Futures basis/carry (#1) [2][3]; EIA inventory surprises for energy (#2) [8]; VRP for equity index (#3) [15]; VIX-term-structure slope (#4) [16]; credit spreads/EBP for activity & risky-bond returns (#5) [18][19]; gold↔real-rates (#6) [12].
- **Medium:** Open interest as a commodity/bond predictor (#9) [4]; copper↔China-PMI (#7) — strong economic logic and co-movement but the cleanest evidence is demand-driven price work rather than a dedicated PMI→return predictive regression [14]; DXY↔commodities (#8) — significant but regime-dependent and unstable post-2020 [5].
- **Low:** MOVE as a *return* predictor (#10) — widely used operationally but lacking strong peer-reviewed return-predictability evidence; best treated as a volatility/risk conditioner. Hedging pressure as a standalone predictor (#11) — contested once basis/inventory are controlled [2][3].

### PIT discipline — the core rules

Distinguish the **reference period** a datum describes from its **public-release timestamp**; lag to the *release*, never the reference date [21]. Croushore & Stark's real-time-data framework — vintages/snapshots of data "available at quarterly intervals in real time" — is the canonical justification, and revised series should ideally be replaced by ALFRED (Archival FRED) vintages to avoid using restated numbers that did not exist at decision time [21]. Practical conventions: (i) apply a **one-day implementation lag (T+1)** to all daily market series (VIX-slope, MOVE, DXY, TIPS/BE10Y, OAS) so a signal computed from day-t closes trades at day t+1; (ii) for **weekly EIA** data, the report is released Wed (petroleum) / Thu (gas) at 10:30 ET and covers the week ending the **prior Friday**, so the inventory level is ~5–6 days stale at release [9][10]; (iii) for **monthly PMI**, NBS prints on the last day of the reference month and Caixin on the first business day of the next month [24]; (iv) for **CFTC positioning/open interest**, data are as-of **Tuesday** but released **Friday 3:30 ET** — a hard 3-day reporting lag, implement the following Monday [22]; (v) **TIPS/BE10Y** (Fed H.15) post at 4:15 ET same day and are subject to later revision, so use T+1 and prefer vintaged values [23].

## Recommendations — staged build for the S1.7 loader

1. **Immediately implementable (daily, T+1):** Build features #3 VRP, #4 VIX-slope, #5 HY/IG OAS, #6 gold↔TIPS/BE10Y + DXY, #8 DXY, #10 MOVE directly from `additional_data.xlsx`. These are real-time market series; a uniform **T+1 close-to-trade lag** removes look-ahead with minimal engineering. Tag each column with its release timestamp in the loader schema.
2. **Calendar-aware features (weekly/monthly):** For #2 EIA, store both the **reference-week-ending Friday** and the **release timestamp**, and forward-fill the value only from the release timestamp onward; compute the surprise against a stored consensus. For #7 China PMI, key the value to NBS month-end / Caixin first-business-day release, then T+1. Build an explicit release-calendar table rather than inferring lags from reference dates — this is where most look-ahead bugs originate.
3. **Source the missing inputs:** Feature #1 (basis/carry) is the highest-value addition and is *not* derivable from front-month OHLCV — prioritise ingesting a 2nd-nearby contract or a precomputed basis series into `additional_data.xlsx`. Similarly ingest CFTC COT/open-interest for #9 with the **Tuesday-as-of / Monday-implementation** rule.
4. **Validation gate:** Before research use, run an ALFRED-vintage spot check on at least TIPS/BE10Y and any revised macro series to confirm the loader is not consuming restated values [21].

**Benchmarks that change these recommendations:** If a 2nd contract cannot be sourced, *demote* commodity cross-sectional carry to time-series momentum on the front contract and lower expected commodity-sleeve Sharpe accordingly. If out-of-sample tests show the VIX-slope or MOVE signal decays to insignificance (e.g., post-fee IR < ~0.2 over a 3-year rolling window), reclassify them from alpha features to pure volatility-targeting conditioners. If the post-2020 DXY↔commodity correlation remains positive/unstable, drop #8 as a standalone signal and retain it only as an interaction term.

## Caveats

- **Regime instability:** The gold↔real-rate and DXY↔commodity relationships have visibly weakened or inverted since 2022 (central-bank gold buying, US net-energy-exporter status) [5][13]; treat coefficients as time-varying.
- **Event vs. continuous prediction:** The EIA evidence is about *announcement-day* price impact and surprise reaction [8], not a slow-moving continuous predictor; the feature is the *surprise*, which requires a consensus series.
- **Positions are contested:** Hong-Yogo find open interest predictive [4], but Gorton-Hayashi-Rouwenhorst and Szymanowska et al. find CFTC positions/hedging pressure do **not** robustly predict premia once inventory/basis is controlled [2][3] — do not over-weight positioning features.
- **MOVE** lacks a strong primary return-predictability citation; its inclusion rests on practitioner/cross-asset-stress usage, so it is flagged Low confidence.
- Some practitioner sources (LBMA, PIMCO, bank desks) were used only to corroborate direction/magnitude; the quantitative anchors above are drawn from peer-reviewed or central-bank primary sources.
