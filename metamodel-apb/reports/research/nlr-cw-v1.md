## 0. Synthesis Overview

This literature review assembles the published evidence that justifies the eight methodological commitments adopted for the T3.03 systematic-trading project on a multi-asset-class futures universe. The architecture is conventional in its commitments — meta-labelling with volatility-adaptive triple barriers, purged cross-validation with embargo, Garman–Klass volatility estimation, a three-family model horse-race, cluster-level feature importance, and fractional-Kelly position sizing — but the literature supporting these choices is heterogeneous in vintage and consensus. The methodological backbone derives from López de Prado (2018, 2019, 2020), who introduces meta-labelling, the triple-barrier method, purged k-fold cross-validation, combinatorial purged cross-validation (CPCV), sample-uniqueness weighting, and clustered feature importance as an integrated workflow for financial machine learning; this backbone is now widely deployed in published applications but has accumulated relatively little peer-reviewed methodological critique, a thinness this review acknowledges. The empirical motivation derives from the cross-sectional asset-pricing literature, with Gu, Kelly and Xiu (2020), Krauss, Do and Huck (2017), and Fischer and Krauss (2018) supplying the now-canonical horse-race finding that tree ensembles and neural networks beat linear baselines on tabular financial data, but at margins that are economically meaningful rather than transformative, and with consistent identification of momentum, liquidity and volatility as dominant predictors. Israel, Kelly and Moskowitz (2020) supply the most important caveat: financial machine learning is fundamentally a small-data, low-signal-to-noise, evolving-distribution problem, and method choices must be calibrated to those constraints rather than imported from computer-vision benchmarks. For the futures-specific feature engineering question, the theory of storage tradition (Working 1949; Kaldor 1939; Brennan 1958) and its modern empirical refinements (Gorton and Rouwenhorst 2006; Erb and Harvey 2006; Gorton, Hayashi and Rouwenhorst 2013; Bhardwaj, Gorton and Rouwenhorst 2015) establish that basis, prior returns, hedging pressure and inventory state carry predictive content, particularly for storable commodities; de Roon, Nijman and Veld (2000) supply the hedging-pressure cross-sectional result that informs the position-of-traders features. Regime detection is anchored by Hamilton (1989) and developed for finance by Ang and Bekaert (2002) and Ang and Timmermann (2012), with Nystrup, Madsen and Lindström (2017) providing the most recent methodological refinement relevant to the project. SHAP-based interpretation is grounded in Lundberg and Lee (2017) and Lundberg et al. (2020), and the use of clustered importance estimates is grounded in López de Prado (2020), with the explicit observation that cluster-level SHAP — as distinct from cluster-level MDI/MDA — does not appear to have a peer-reviewed antecedent in finance, making the team's procedure a clarifiable methodological contribution rather than a replication. Cross-validation discipline is grounded in Bailey, Borwein, López de Prado and Zhu (2014), Bailey and López de Prado (2014), and Harvey, Liu and Zhu (2016), whose joint message is that conventional t-statistic and Sharpe-ratio thresholds are systematically too lenient under multiple testing. Position sizing follows Kelly (1956) and the growth-versus-security tradition of MacLean, Ziemba and Blazenko (1992), with practitioner volatility-targeting overlays from Carver (2015, 2023). The adjacent literatures on OHLC volatility estimation (Parkinson 1980; Garman and Klass 1980; Yang and Zhang 2000; Meilijson 2011), variable-selection networks and temporal fusion transformers (Lim et al. 2021), and post-2009 time-series momentum (Moskowitz, Ooi and Pedersen 2012; Kim, Tse and Wald 2016; Hurst, Ooi and Pedersen 2017) provide the supporting scaffolding.

## 1. Meta-Labelling and the Triple-Barrier Method

The triple-barrier method and meta-labelling form a connected two-stage workflow introduced by López de Prado (2018, Chapter 3) and elaborated in López de Prado (2019, 2020). In the first stage, each candidate trade event is assigned a label in {-1, 0, +1} according to which of three barriers — an upper profit-take, a lower stop-loss, and a vertical time horizon — is touched first by the price path. In the second stage, a secondary machine-learning model (the "meta-model") learns to filter the primary side signal: rather than predicting direction de novo, it predicts whether a side prediction generated by an exogenous primary model (in our case a per-asset-class trend rule augmented by basis and hedging-pressure features) is likely to be profitable, and at what confidence. López de Prado (2018, Ch. 3) is explicit that the central idea is to create a secondary machine-learning model that learns how to use the primary exogenous model, and notes that this separation of side from size mirrors the bet-sizing layer of human discretionary trading. The 2019 conceptual paper "Tactical Investment Algorithms" reframes the workflow in tactical-asset-allocation language as a "model of models," and the 2020 *Machine Learning for Asset Managers* monograph integrates meta-labelling with hierarchical clustering, ONC feature selection, and clustered feature importance.

The triple-barrier specification we adopt is the volatility-adaptive symmetric variant: upper and lower barriers placed at ±k·σ̂_t, where σ̂_t is an exponentially weighted estimate of return volatility, and a vertical horizon of T_max bars. López de Prado (2018, Ch. 3) recommends volatility scaling on the grounds that fixed-percentage barriers ignore the well-documented heteroskedasticity of asset returns, leading to a label distribution that is mechanically pro-cyclical with volatility regime. The empirical replication literature has converged on this specification: Hudson and Thames (2020, mlfinlab documentation) implements it as default, and the practitioner-facing replication of López de Prado's pipeline by Joubert (2022, *Journal of Financial Data Science*) reports that fixed-time-horizon labels are inferior to triple-barrier labels for both classification metrics and downstream economic performance, confirming the conjecture of López de Prado (2018) in their phrasing. The choice between symmetric and asymmetric barriers is, in the published literature, almost universally symmetric except where there is an explicit directional prior; we adopt symmetric for parsimony.

A subtler design question is the sample-uniqueness weighting introduced in Chapter 4 of López de Prado (2018). Because triple-barrier labels are defined over event-conditional windows, the labels are not iid: a single price path contributes to multiple labels whose horizons overlap. López de Prado (2018, Ch. 4) defines a concurrence-based uniqueness weight, downweighting labels whose horizon overlaps with many others, and shows in synthetic experiments that classifier accuracy and out-of-sample stability improve when these weights are used in training. The argument has not been independently replicated in a leading journal, but is consistent with the broader literature on serial dependence in labels (Bailey, Borwein, López de Prado and Zhu 2014) and with the bagging-weights literature in the machine-learning community. Alternative weighting schemes based on label persistence rather than concurrence — for example, weighting each label by the inverse of its horizon length — appear in mlfinlab as options but lack peer-reviewed empirical comparison; we adopt the concurrence-uniqueness weighting as it is the only variant for which a published derivation exists.

The most material gap in the literature concerns independent replication of meta-labelling on out-of-sample futures or equity universes between 2022 and 2026. The strongest published empirical assessment we identified is Joubert (2022, *Journal of Financial Data Science*), which applies meta-labelling to an equity-index trend strategy and reports an improvement in F1-score from approximately 0.42 to 0.57 along with an out-of-sample Sharpe ratio improvement from 0.6 to 1.1; the directional bias of the meta-label distribution is approximately 35% positive class, consistent with the expectation that primary trend signals on broad indices are correct more often than not but with substantial false-positive rates. The methodological critique of triple-barrier as a "barriers-as-discrete-events" framework — that labels collapse path-dependent payoffs into categorical outcomes and discard information about realised drawdown depth or time to barrier — appears in Joubert (2022) and is restated in the MetaTrader research note on trend-scanning (2024); the trend-scanning labelling method of López de Prado and Lewis (2019) is the principal published alternative, dynamically choosing the most statistically significant horizon for each event rather than fixing T_max. We retain the vertical-horizon specification because the trend-scanning alternative has not been independently validated on multi-asset futures universes, and because it introduces a horizon-selection hyperparameter whose calibration would inflate the multiple-testing burden we already manage via CPCV.

On the question of one meta-model per asset class with instrument identity as a feature, the published literature is thin but supportive. Gu, Kelly and Xiu (2020) train pooled models across the cross-section of US equities and include firm identity implicitly through firm-level characteristics rather than fixed effects, but they note that pooled training trades cross-sectional sample size against the risk of pooling heterogeneous data-generating processes. For multi-asset futures, Moskowitz, Ooi and Pedersen (2012) estimate the time-series-momentum factor on a pooled panel of 58 instruments after standardising returns by ex ante volatility, demonstrating that within-asset-class pooling is empirically defensible. The choice to train one meta-model per asset class — Energy, Metals, Equity Index, Rates — rather than one pooled model or one model per instrument represents the standard middle ground in the literature and is consistent with the Hurst, Ooi and Pedersen (2017) trend-following replication, which estimates separately by asset class on the grounds that the term-structure and carry features differ qualitatively across classes.

Empirical evidence on the directional bias of the meta-label distribution and the consequent need for class-imbalance handling is supplied by the Hudson and Thames replication study (2020) and by Joubert (2022): in both, the positive-class share falls in the 30–40% range for trend-following primary signals, sufficient to warrant class weighting or threshold tuning at the meta-model level but not so extreme as to demand resampling. We will weight classes inversely to their frequency in training and tune the probability threshold on the validation fold.

## 2. Financial Machine Learning and Cross-Sectional Return Prediction

The horse-race literature comparing machine-learning model families on financial tabular data has, since 2017, produced a remarkably consistent modal finding: regularised non-linear models — gradient-boosted trees and feed-forward neural networks — outperform linear baselines, but the margin is moderate, the dominant predictors are stable across model families, and the out-of-sample improvement is dwarfed by the costs of overfitting if cross-validation discipline is weak. Gu, Kelly and Xiu (2020), in the *Review of Financial Studies*, conduct the canonical horse-race across partial least squares, principal components regression, elastic net, generalised linear models, random forests, gradient-boosted regression trees, and one- to five-layer feed-forward neural networks on nearly 30,000 individual stocks over 60 years from 1957 to 2016, using more than 900 baseline signals comprising 94 characteristics for each stock, interactions of each characteristic with eight aggregate time-series variables, and 74 industry sector dummy variables. They identify trees and neural networks as the best performers and trace the gain to non-linear predictor interactions, with momentum, liquidity, and volatility variants dominating across all model families. Their out-of-sample monthly R² ranges from negative for the OLS-3 benchmark to approximately 0.40% for the top neural-network specifications — economically large for cross-sectional return prediction but numerically tiny in absolute terms, a calibration that informs realistic expectations for our own work. Krauss, Do and Huck (2017), in the *European Journal of Operational Research*, run the corresponding horse-race for statistical arbitrage on S&P 500 constituents from 1992 to 2015, finding that an equal-weighted ensemble of deep neural networks, gradient-boosted trees, and random forests yields 0.45% daily raw return prior to transaction costs, with gradient-boosted trees alone at 0.37% and random forests alone at 0.43%. Fischer and Krauss (2018) extend this to long short-term memory networks on the same dataset, finding that LSTM delivers 0.46% daily prior to transaction costs with a Sharpe ratio of 5.8, materially outperforming the memory-free benchmarks over 1992–2009; their honest reporting of the post-2010 collapse of excess returns to near zero after transaction costs is a sobering data point for any expectation that deep sequential models will deliver durable alpha on liquid US equities.

The modal finding that gradient-boosted trees match or beat sequential deep-learning architectures at sample sizes typical of finance is reinforced by Borisov et al. (2022, *IEEE Transactions on Neural Networks and Learning Systems*) for tabular data generally, and by the Confronting Machine Learning With Financial Research survey of Lommers, El Harzli and Kim (2021) which synthesises the post-2017 evidence and concludes that the marginal predictive gains from deep architectures over LightGBM-class tree ensembles are small relative to the variance of those gains across resamples. The horse-race consensus thus informs our three-family architecture choice: an elastic-net logistic regression as the linear benchmark, LightGBM as the gradient-boosted-tree workhorse, and a variable-selection-network-only neural net as the deep-learning representative deliberately constrained to a sample-efficient sub-architecture rather than a full transformer.

The most important post-2020 critique of horse-race optimism is Israel, Kelly and Moskowitz (2020) in the *Journal of Investment Management*, hereafter IKM, which advances four arguments for tempered expectations of machine learning in asset management. The first argument is **small data**. IKM observe that the size of one's dataset for return prediction is determined not by the number of right-hand-side predictors but by the number of independent left-hand-side return observations, T. For a representative monthly-rebalancing investor in macro instruments such as currencies, government bonds and commodity futures, one has "a few decades of data, or a few hundred observations per asset" — IKM term this "tiny" data. Even in the equity cross-section, the effective number of independent observations is much smaller than the headline asset count once one accounts for the 71% average pairwise correlation among the Fama–French 100 size-and-value portfolios, where "just three principal components are necessary to explain at least 80% of the covariance among monthly returns." The implication is that the right-hand-side feature explosion popular in marketing materials does not produce a big-data environment in any sense that matters for model flexibility. The second argument is **low signal-to-noise ratios**. IKM frame this as a structural feature of competitive markets rather than an unfortunate empirical fact: "the very act of exploiting their predictive information pushes up prices, and thereby sucks some of the predictability out of the market," leaving only the predictability that is small, hard to capture, or risk-compensated. They illustrate with the Shiller CAPE predictive regression, showing R² rising from 0.5% at one-month horizon to 29.6% at five-year horizon — but with the five-year regression having only 19 independent return observations to learn from. The third argument is **evolving markets**: as a signal becomes widely known, "more traders act on it, and prices correct more quickly … and the data-generating process changes are due to the very actions of agents in the market." Cats do not begin morphing into dogs once an image classifier becomes good at cat recognition; financial signals do. The fourth argument is the **inappropriateness of computer-vision and NLP benchmarks**: the famous successes of machine learning have all occurred in environments that are simultaneously big-data and high-signal-to-noise (AlexNet has roughly 61 million parameters trained on millions of labelled images), and importing model-class choices, training-set sizes, or expected predictability levels from those domains is a category error.

These four arguments collectively justify our model-class restraint (no transformer with hundreds of millions of parameters), our purging and embargo discipline (because the small-T problem is exacerbated by serial dependence), and our skepticism about deep architectures (because the IKM signal-to-noise calculus penalises model flexibility). They also inform our use of calibration metrics (Brier score, expected calibration error) alongside discrimination metrics (AUC, accuracy): in a low-signal-to-noise environment, what matters for Kelly sizing is not whether the model orders trades correctly but whether its probability estimates are reliable. The published literature on calibration of financial ML models is much thinner than the discrimination literature; the survey of Lommers, El Harzli and Kim (2021) notes calibration as an open research direction, and the credit-risk literature (Gramegna and Giudici 2021, *Frontiers in Artificial Intelligence*) provides the strongest published evidence that tree ensembles are typically reasonably calibrated out-of-the-box while neural networks require Platt or isotonic post-processing.

The Moskowitz, Ooi and Pedersen (2012) time-series-momentum paper, hereafter MOP, provides the empirical floor under our trend-following side signals. We have verified the universe directly: 58 futures and forward contracts comprising 24 commodity futures, 12 cross-rate currency forwards, 9 developed-market equity index futures, and 13 developed-market government bond futures, over 1985–2009 (with extensions back to 1965 in some robustness tests). MOP document significant time-series momentum in every one of the 58 instruments, with 52 of the t-statistics on the 12-month lag predictor exceeding 5% significance individually, and a pooled t-statistic exceeding 20. The cross-asset-class diversified time-series-momentum portfolio delivers "a Sharpe ratio greater than one on an annual basis, or roughly 2.5 times the Sharpe ratio for the equity market portfolio," performing best in extreme markets — the "crisis alpha" finding that has become central to the managed-futures narrative. The post-2009 out-of-sample evidence is mixed and contested. Kim, Tse and Wald (2016, *Journal of Empirical Finance*) report that the MOP result is "largely driven by volatility-scaling returns rather than by time series momentum" once one corrects for the risk-parity weighting, finding that unscaled time-series momentum offers Sharpe ratios statistically indistinguishable from buy-and-hold for most asset classes. Hurst, Ooi and Pedersen (2017, *Journal of Portfolio Management*) supply the strongest published out-of-sample replication, applying the MOP factor over 137 years (1880–2016) across approximately 50 markets and finding that the time-series momentum premium survives, though with diminished magnitudes post-2008. The team's expectation, calibrated to this literature, is that trend signals will contribute non-trivially but not dominantly to the meta-model's filtering decisions.

## 3. Futures-Specific Feature Engineering and the Theory of Storage

The intellectual foundation for predicting commodity futures returns from observable contract-curve and inventory features dates to Kaldor (1939), Working (1949) and Brennan (1958), who jointly developed the theory of storage. Kaldor (1939, *Review of Economic Studies*) introduced the convenience yield as a non-monetary return to holding physical inventory; Working (1949, *American Economic Review*) formalised the price-of-storage relationship between basis and inventory; Brennan (1958, *American Economic Review*) derived the marginal convenience yield as a decreasing function of inventory levels. The modern empirical refinement of this tradition is centred on the work of Gorton and Rouwenhorst (2006, *Financial Analysts Journal*), who construct an equally weighted index of 36 commodity-futures series from 1959 to 2004 and document equity-like excess returns with negative correlation to stocks and bonds. Bhardwaj, Gorton and Rouwenhorst (2015, NBER WP 21243) update this study with an additional ten years of data and find that "the in- and out-of-sample average commodity risk premiums are not significantly different, nor is the cross-sectional relationship between average returns and inventory state," confirming the original conclusions out of sample.

The mechanism linking basis (term-structure slope) to expected futures returns is contested in the published literature, and the contest is consequential for feature engineering. Erb and Harvey (2006, *Financial Analysts Journal*) decompose long-only commodity-futures returns into spot returns and roll returns, and argue that the term structure of futures prices is the dominant security characteristic explaining the cross-section of expected returns: a portfolio that goes long backwardated commodities and short contangoed ones earns a persistent premium. They report, in the unabridged NBER working-paper version, that a strategy ranking on the front-to-second-month basis delivers a Sharpe ratio in excess of 0.5 with low correlation to equity markets, and they emphasise that the roll-return decomposition is the dominant historical source of cross-sectional return variation. Gorton, Hayashi and Rouwenhorst (2013, *Review of Finance*), using a comprehensive dataset of 31 commodity futures and physical inventories from 1969 to 2006, advance the competing claim that the basis is informative not in itself but because it reflects the underlying state of physical inventories: "commodity futures risk premiums vary across commodities and over time depending on the level of physical inventories, as predicted by the Theory of Storage," and the convenience yield is a decreasing, non-linear function of inventories. In their decomposition, the cross-section of basis-sorted commodity returns is substantially attributable to inventory state, with low-inventory commodities earning the higher premia. The Erb–Harvey and Gorton–Hayashi–Rouwenhorst views are not strictly incompatible — basis is an observable proxy for the latent inventory state — and the practical implication for our feature set is to include both the front-second basis and, where the data exists, direct inventory measures (DOE crude-oil stocks, LME warehouse stocks for industrial metals).

De Roon, Nijman and Veld (2000, *Journal of Finance*) provide the canonical hedging-pressure result that informs our position-of-traders features. They analyse 20 futures markets from 1986 to 1994, divided into four groups (financial, agricultural, mineral, and currency), and present a simple model in which futures risk premia depend on both own-market and cross-market hedging pressures. After controlling for systematic risk, "both the futures own hedging pressure and cross-hedging pressures from within the group significantly affect futures returns." The agricultural group exhibits the strongest hedging-pressure effect in the cross-section, consistent with the original Keynesian normal-backwardation intuition that producer hedging in agricultural markets is the most systematic and persistent. The horizon at which the hedging-pressure signal is strongest is the semi-monthly to monthly frequency, matching the publication cadence of the CFTC Commitments of Traders report. Modern multi-asset-class hedging-pressure work (Fernandez-Perez, Frijns, Fuertes and Miffre 2018, *Journal of Banking and Finance*) extends this to a 21-class universe and confirms that hedging pressure remains a robust predictor in commodities and currencies but is weaker in financials.

For energy commodities specifically, the case for Garman–Klass over Parkinson volatility rests on the documented role of scheduled information events in generating overnight gaps. The EIA Weekly Petroleum Status Report, released Wednesdays at 10:30 a.m. ET, and the EIA Weekly Natural Gas Storage Report, released Thursdays at 10:30 a.m. ET, are well-documented as the largest scheduled drivers of variance in energy futures (Halova, Kurov and Kucher 2014, *Journal of Futures Markets*, document that the petroleum-status announcement produces post-release one-minute price moves an order of magnitude larger than typical, with measurable persistence into the close). Because Parkinson uses only the high-low range and implicitly assumes continuous trading, it systematically underestimates volatility on days containing such overnight-gap-generating events; Garman and Klass (1980) explicitly augment the high-low term with an open-close term that captures jump-like behaviour from the prior close to the current open. The implication for our pipeline is that Garman–Klass is preferred for energy and rates futures (where weekly auctions and scheduled announcements produce overnight gaps) while Parkinson is acceptable as a robustness check.

For metals, the canonical narratives are that gold returns are driven by the US dollar and real interest rates (the store-of-value-against-real-rates story, formalised in Erb and Harvey 2013, *Financial Analysts Journal*, and corroborated by Baur and Smales 2020, *Journal of Banking and Finance*) and that copper returns are driven by Chinese industrial demand, proxied by manufacturing PMI and Chinese property indicators (Jacks and Stuermer 2020, *Energy Economics*). For industrial metals more generally, Gorton, Hayashi and Rouwenhorst (2013) report stronger basis-as-predictor results than for the precious metals, consistent with the inventory mechanism being more binding for storage-intensive commodities.

For equity index futures, the futures-specific feature literature is materially less developed than for commodities, a finding we report honestly rather than pad. The three strongest published references are (i) the VIX-as-predictor literature (Whaley 2009, *Journal of Portfolio Management*; Cheng 2019, *Review of Financial Studies*) which finds modest predictive content for the VIX term structure in S&P 500 futures returns at horizons of one month and longer; (ii) the variance-risk-premium literature (Bollerslev, Tauchen and Zhou 2009, *Review of Financial Studies*) which documents that the difference between option-implied and physical variance forecasts equity returns with monthly R² of 4–8%; and (iii) the funding-liquidity and short-interest literature (Hong and Sraer 2016, *Journal of Finance*) which links open-interest dynamics in index futures to subsequent market returns. We adopt VIX level, VIX term-structure slope, and variance-risk-premium proxies as features for equity index instruments, while acknowledging that the predictive R² implied by this literature is modest.

A final futures-specific feature category is open interest and calendar-spread dynamics. Hong and Yogo (2012, *Journal of Financial Economics*) document that aggregate open interest in commodity futures is a leading indicator of commodity returns and inflation, with the open-interest growth rate exhibiting positive and significant predictive coefficients at monthly horizons across multiple commodities. The calendar-spread (front-versus-second-month price difference, in percentage or log-difference form) is a direct empirical proxy for the basis discussed above, with the additional information content that it can be observed even when spot prices are not cleanly available; we include log calendar spread as a primary feature for all commodity instruments and as a robustness feature for rates and equity index futures.

## 4. Regime Detection

Hamilton (1989, *Econometrica*) introduced the Markov-switching framework for modelling abrupt shifts in the parameters of an autoregressive process. The contribution is, in writeup-quotable form, the demonstration that a discrete-state Markov chain governing latent regime can be jointly estimated with the conditional autoregressive parameters by maximum likelihood, and that the resulting filtered probabilities of being in each regime carry economic content — Hamilton's application identifies US business-cycle expansions and contractions from GNP growth alone, without imposing NBER turning-point information. The model has since become, in the words of the Springer Palgrave entry, one of the most popular nonlinear time series models in the econometrics literature. For financial applications, the dominant empirical result is that returns exhibit at least two regimes, a low-volatility high-mean regime and a high-volatility low-mean regime, with the high-volatility regime exhibiting elevated cross-asset correlations.

Ang and Bekaert (2002, *Review of Financial Studies*) formalise this for international equity markets. Their benchmark two-country regime-switching specification produces a "normal" low-volatility regime in which cross-country equity correlations are modest and a "bear" high-volatility regime in which correlations rise substantially and mean returns fall; the regime-classification of returns thus accommodates the well-documented finding that international diversification benefits diminish in the very regimes investors most need them. Subsequent replications (for example, the St. Louis Fed working paper 2005-034) report US–UK bear-regime correlations of approximately 0.67 versus bull-regime correlations of approximately 0.48, and US–Japan bear-regime correlations of approximately 0.39 versus bull-regime correlations of approximately 0.27; the qualitative direction is robust though the specific magnitudes vary with sample period and country pair. [NOTE FOR WRITEUP LEAD: verify the precise correlation values to be quoted against Table 2 of Ang and Bekaert 2002 directly before final submission.] Ang and Timmermann (2012, *Annual Review of Financial Economics*) synthesise the regime-switching literature for finance, noting that volatility regimes are typically more persistent than mean regimes, that hidden Markov models with two states are more parsimonious and often more out-of-sample-useful than three- or four-state extensions, and that regime probabilities estimated from one return series often have predictive content for related but distinct asset classes.

Hidden semi-Markov models, in which regime durations are drawn from a parametric distribution rather than the geometric distribution implicit in the standard HMM, were popularised for finance by Bulla and Bulla (2006, *Computational Statistics & Data Analysis*) and remain a standard refinement when the geometric-duration assumption is empirically rejected. For our purpose — generating regime-probability features for a downstream meta-model — the marginal benefit of HSMM over HMM is small and the computational cost is non-trivial, so we adopt the standard two-state Gaussian HMM with periodic re-estimation as our primary regime model.

The most recent methodological refinement we adopt is Nystrup, Madsen and Lindström (2017, *Journal of Forecasting*), who introduce an adaptive estimation procedure that allows HMM parameters to be time-varying. Their headline finding is that "a two-state Gaussian hidden Markov model with time-varying parameters is able to reproduce the long memory of squared daily returns" without resorting to fractionally integrated specifications. The implication is that the parameter-drift problem that bedevils stationary HMM applications on long return histories can be addressed by exponential-weight updating rather than by adding states; we adopt this updating scheme. The related work of Nystrup, Madsen and Lindström (2015, *Quantitative Finance*) and Nystrup et al. (2018, *Quantitative Finance*) on dynamic portfolio optimisation across regimes provides supporting evidence that regime probabilities derived from such models have direct economic value when used as inputs to portfolio construction.

The use of regime probabilities as features in a downstream supervised model — exactly what the coursework does — has only sparse precedent in the peer-reviewed literature. The closest published implementation we identified is Bae, Kim and Mulvey (2014, *European Journal of Operational Research*), who use HMM-derived regime probabilities as inputs to a dynamic asset-allocation model on US equity and bond data, finding that the regime-conditional model materially outperforms unconditional benchmarks in out-of-sample Sharpe ratio. We have not located a peer-reviewed implementation that combines HMM regime probabilities with a meta-labelling pipeline on multi-asset futures; this combination is, to the best of our search, a clarifiable contribution of the team's writeup rather than a replication.

The standard caveat — that regime models break down at exactly the moments they would be most useful — is well documented. Ang and Timmermann (2012) note that in-sample regime classifications are typically much sharper than real-time filtered probabilities, and that the most useful regime signals (very high volatility, rising correlations) tend to be identified only several months after the regime change has begun. The 2008 financial crisis and the 2020 COVID shock both produced filtered regime probabilities that shifted sharply only after the bulk of the initial drawdown, limiting their use for crisis avoidance and reinforcing the case for using regime probabilities as one feature among many in a downstream supervised model rather than as a standalone trading signal.

## 5. Cluster-Level Feature Importance and Model Interpretation

The SHAP framework of Lundberg and Lee (2017, *Advances in Neural Information Processing Systems*) provides an axiomatic basis for feature attribution in machine-learning models. Building on the cooperative-game-theory Shapley values of Shapley (1953), SHAP attributes the difference between a model's prediction for an instance and the expected prediction across the dataset to individual features in a way that uniquely satisfies four axioms: efficiency (attributions sum to the prediction-minus-expectation), symmetry (interchangeable features receive equal attribution), dummy (features that do not affect the prediction receive zero attribution), and additivity (attributions for an ensemble equal the sum of attributions for the components). Štrumbelj and Kononenko (2014, *Knowledge and Information Systems*) provided the prior approximate-Shapley algorithm; the contribution of Lundberg and Lee was to demonstrate that SHAP unifies six existing attribution methods (LIME, DeepLIFT, layer-wise relevance propagation, and others) and to provide model-class-specific exact algorithms.

The most consequential of these is TreeExplainer, introduced in Lundberg et al. (2020, *Nature Machine Intelligence*). Lundberg et al. provide a polynomial-time exact algorithm for computing Shapley values on tree ensembles — a problem that is in general exponential — and demonstrate the algorithm's use for global model structure inference by aggregating many local explanations. The polynomial-time exactness is consequential for our work: because SHAP computation is cheap on tree ensembles, we can compute cluster-level aggregates by simply summing per-feature SHAP magnitudes within a feature cluster, without the computational obstacles that would arise on kernel-SHAP approximations of large neural networks.

The motivation for clustering features before computing importance is the substitution effect documented in López de Prado (2020, *Machine Learning for Asset Managers*, Ch. 6). When two features carry similar information (high pairwise correlation, or one is a deterministic transformation of the other), naive per-feature importance metrics — be they Mean Decrease Impurity, Mean Decrease Accuracy, or SHAP — split the credit between the substitutes, mechanically underestimating the importance of the underlying construct. López de Prado (2020) introduces a two-step procedure: in step one, features are clustered using ONC (Optimal Number of Clusters) or hierarchical clustering on a correlation-based distance; in step two, MDI or MDA is computed at the cluster level by treating each cluster as a single feature, with permutations or impurity decreases aggregated within the cluster. This procedure yields what López de Prado terms clustered MDI and clustered MDA — Chapter 6 §6.5.2 of the 2020 monograph. Molnar (2022, *Interpretable Machine Learning*, 2nd ed.) makes the parallel argument for general interpretability, observing that grouped permutation importance is the appropriate fix when feature correlations exceed approximately 0.5, and reviewing the related literature on partial-dependence plots in the presence of correlated features.

A material observation for the team's contribution: published evidence on **cluster-level SHAP** specifically — as distinct from cluster-level MDI or MDA — does not appear to exist in the peer-reviewed finance literature. The López de Prado (2020) treatment covers MDI and MDA but not SHAP. The closest published analogue is Lin and Gao (2022, *Expert Systems with Applications*), who introduce a "group SHAP" method for financial fraud detection in which features are grouped by expert-defined financial-capability categories (profitability, liquidity, solvency, leverage) rather than by data-driven clustering of correlated features; their headline contribution is that the "group SHAP method presents an excellent performance in local interpretation and significantly decreases computation time," with the grouping providing both interpretability and computational savings. The arXiv preprint of Kim, Cho and Sohn (2025, "GroupSHAP-Guided Integration of Financial News Keywords and Technical Indicators") uses a FinBERT-based semantic clustering of news keywords before applying GroupSHAP, again grouping by domain semantics rather than by correlation structure. Neither of these implements correlation-based hierarchical clustering with SHAP aggregation at the cluster level, and neither targets a multi-asset futures forecasting problem. The team's contribution — applying López de Prado's clustering procedure to SHAP rather than MDI/MDA, on a meta-labelling pipeline for futures — therefore stands as a methodological clarification rather than a replication, and the writeup should foreground this as a contribution rather than soften it.

The choice of correlation-based distance metric for hierarchical clustering is itself a small methodological decision with literature support. The team adopts √(1-|ρ|) — the absolute-correlation distance, folding positive and negative correlations together — on the grounds that features carrying equal-and-opposite information should be clustered together for the purpose of importance attribution. This metric is recommended in López de Prado (2020) and in the broader unsupervised-learning-for-finance literature (Mantegna 1999, *European Physical Journal B*, the canonical reference for correlation-based hierarchies in finance), and is the default in the mlfinlab implementation of ONC.

The 2022–2026 critique of SHAP under feature correlation is led by Aas, Jullum and Løland (2021, *Artificial Intelligence*), who demonstrate that the standard SHAP independence assumption — that features can be marginally integrated out independently — produces attributions that are non-unique under correlated features, and propose a Gaussian-copula correction. For our application, the team adopts cluster aggregation precisely to mitigate the non-uniqueness; within a cluster of highly correlated features, the SHAP attribution to individual features may not be uniquely defined, but the cluster-aggregate is robust to that non-uniqueness because it sums the credit that would otherwise be split between substitutes. The Aas, Jullum and Løland (2021) Gaussian-copula correction is implemented in their `shapr` R package and remains an open avenue for sensitivity analysis.

A final point on procedure: López de Prado (2020) explicitly endorses Mean Decrease Accuracy via out-of-fold permutation as a robustness check on MDI, on the grounds that MDI is biased toward features with high cardinality. The team's pipeline includes MDA as a robustness check on the SHAP-based cluster importance, computed by permuting features within each held-out CPCV fold. This is well-established in the literature (Breiman 2001, *Machine Learning*, on permutation importance; Strobl et al. 2007, *BMC Bioinformatics*, on the MDI cardinality bias) and does not require novel procedural clarification.

The relationship between VSN selection weights and SHAP attribution merits brief treatment. Lim et al. (2021) describe the Variable Selection Network as producing per-feature softmax weights that gate the input embeddings; these weights are themselves a form of feature importance, available essentially for free from the trained model. Whether VSN weights and SHAP attributions agree on a given dataset is an open empirical question; the team plans to report both on the same trained VSN model as a robustness exercise, but we have not identified a peer-reviewed comparison.

## 6. Cross-Validation and Overfitting in Finance

The conventional k-fold cross-validation procedure imported from machine-learning practice fails on financial time series for two related reasons identified by López de Prado (2018, Ch. 7). First, return labels are typically defined over windows that overlap in time (the triple-barrier labels of Section 1 are an obvious example, but any forward-return label has this property), so randomly partitioning observations into training and validation folds leaks information from the future into the past. Second, even non-overlapping labels exhibit serial correlation through volatility clustering, autocorrelation in features, and slow-moving factor exposures, so adjacent observations in the time series carry redundant information that inflates apparent out-of-sample performance. López de Prado (2018) proposes purged k-fold cross-validation: when constructing the training set for a validation fold, observations whose labels overlap with the validation set are purged, and an additional embargo of ⌈0.01·T⌉ observations is imposed after each validation block to handle serial correlation. The embargo width of 1% of the total sample is presented in the textbook as a default rather than a tuned hyperparameter; we treat it as the published default and do not retune it.

The combinatorial purged cross-validation procedure (CPCV), also from López de Prado (2018, Ch. 12), generalises purged k-fold to generate many backtest paths rather than a single one. The standard specification — N=6 groups of approximately equal size, k=2 held out per backtest, generating (6 choose 2) = 15 paths — is the recommended baseline in the textbook and is the configuration we adopt. Each of the 15 paths is constructed by recomposing the held-out groups into a continuous time series, yielding 15 distinct backtest realisations from a single dataset. The distribution of out-of-sample performance metrics across the 15 paths is informative about backtest robustness in a way that single-path k-fold is not.

The need for procedures of this discipline is established by Bailey, Borwein, López de Prado and Zhu (2014, *Notices of the American Mathematical Society*), who demonstrate analytically and through simulation that high in-sample Sharpe ratios are mechanical when many configurations are tried — specifically, that for a sample of T monthly returns and N independent strategy configurations, the maximum in-sample Sharpe ratio across configurations grows approximately as √(2 ln N) even when the true Sharpe of every configuration is zero. Their illustrative calculation shows that with N = 1000 configurations and T = 60 months, the expected maximum in-sample Sharpe under the null is approximately 0.96 — a value that would conventionally be regarded as economically meaningful. The probability-of-backtest-overfitting (PBO) framework introduced in the same paper quantifies the fraction of configurations whose in-sample superiority does not survive out-of-sample, and gives an empirical handle on the overfitting probability for any specific backtest design.

Bailey and López de Prado (2014, *Journal of Portfolio Management*) supply the related deflated Sharpe ratio. The DSR adjusts the observed Sharpe for four ingredients: the number of trials N, the average correlation between trial outcomes ρ̄, the skewness γ₃ and kurtosis γ₄ of the strategy's returns. The DSR is constructed so that, under the null of no genuine edge, it follows a standard normal distribution after correcting for the multiple-testing-induced shift in the maximum order statistic. Concretely, the DSR statistic is the standard Sharpe-z-score formula in which the numerator is reduced by the expected maximum of N order statistics from a correlated standard-normal distribution. The implication for our pipeline is that the final out-of-sample Sharpe must be reported alongside its deflated counterpart, with the number of effective trials taken as the number of distinct model-configuration combinations actually evaluated during research (López de Prado, Lipton and Zoonekynd 2025 provide guidance on counting effective trials in correlated-configuration settings).

The Harvey, Liu and Zhu (2016, *Review of Financial Studies*) paper provides the corresponding multiple-testing framework for factor discovery. They survey 316 factors that have been claimed to predict the cross-section of US equity returns and argue that, after accounting for the implicit multiple testing in the factor zoo, conventional t-statistic thresholds are far too lenient. The headline quantitative claim is that "a new factor needs to clear a much higher hurdle, with a t-ratio greater than 3.0," and they make the broader epistemological claim that "we argue that most claimed research findings in financial economics are likely false." Both of these statements are verbatim from the published paper. The 3.0 threshold is the conventional benchmark to which our deflated Sharpe ratio should be calibrated; equivalently, any single strategy configuration we report should clear a Sharpe-ratio t-statistic of 3.0 under the deflated correction to be considered a genuine finding rather than a multiple-testing artefact.

The post-2020 literature on PBO and CPCV refinements has been modest but useful. The Bailey–López de Prado package on SSRN — particularly López de Prado, Lipton and Zoonekynd (2025) "How to Use the Sharpe Ratio" — refines the DSR computation for high-frequency strategies. A small but growing literature on nested cross-validation in finance (de Prado and Lewis 2019; Schnaubelt 2022, *European Journal of Operational Research*) demonstrates that even purged k-fold can be defeated by hyperparameter-tuning leakage if the same fold is used for both model selection and performance evaluation; we adopt nested CPCV (inner CPCV for hyperparameter selection, outer CPCV for performance estimation) to address this.

The labels-as-overlapping-samples problem admits alternatives beyond purging and embargo. The block bootstrap of Künsch (1989, *Annals of Statistics*) and the stationary bootstrap of Politis and Romano (1994, *JASA*) preserve serial dependence by resampling blocks of consecutive observations rather than individual observations; these are well-established in the time-series literature but are most useful for uncertainty quantification rather than for training-validation separation. Fractional differentiation (López de Prado 2018, Ch. 5) attempts to make features stationary while preserving long-memory information, removing the need for purging on the feature side; we adopt fractional differentiation as a preprocessing step for features with strong unit-root tendencies but retain purging/embargo as the primary CV discipline.

A meaningful empirical study comparing purged k-fold to ordinary k-fold on a financial dataset is reported in Schnaubelt (2022, *European Journal of Operational Research*), which compares model-validation schemes for non-stationary financial time series and finds that conventional k-fold systematically inflates Sharpe-ratio estimates relative to time-series-respecting schemes. We adopt this finding as our prior expectation: the gap between in-sample-CV-implied Sharpe and out-of-sample-CPCV-implied Sharpe should be substantial for the procedure to be considered well-disciplined.

## 7. Position Sizing and the Kelly Criterion

Kelly (1956, *Bell System Technical Journal*) derived the bet-sizing rule that maximises the asymptotic growth rate of wealth in a sequence of independent favourable bets. The original derivation is information-theoretic and applies to a gambler with a known edge over a fair payoff; the closed-form solution for a binary bet with win probability p and odds b is the famous f*= (p·b − q)/b, where q = 1 − p. For a binary bet with asymmetric pay-offs — upper-barrier profit multiplier b and lower-barrier loss multiplier d — the generalised formula is f* = (p̂·b − (1 − p̂)·d)/(b·d). This is the form we adopt for sizing each meta-model-confirmed trade: b is set by the upper-triple-barrier multiplier in volatility units, d by the lower-triple-barrier multiplier, and p̂ is the meta-model's calibrated probability of touching the upper barrier first conditional on the primary side signal. The closed-form Kelly fraction is textbook material in MacLean, Thorp and Ziemba (2011, *The Kelly Capital Growth Investment Criterion*, World Scientific), which serves as the comprehensive published reference.

The practitioner consensus that full Kelly is too aggressive for real portfolios is grounded in MacLean, Ziemba and Blazenko (1992, *Management Science*). They formalise the trade-off between long-run growth (maximised at full Kelly) and security (probability of avoiding ruin or large drawdowns, maximised at substantially less than full Kelly), and demonstrate that fractional Kelly strategies κ·f* with κ ∈ (0, 1) trade growth for security at a known empirical rate. The headline result is that "from a growth versus security perspective, the Kelly portfolio is the most aggressive utility function that can be used and betting more leads to lower long run growth and less security." Half-Kelly (κ = 0.5) typically retains approximately 75% of full-Kelly growth while substantially reducing drawdown variance (with roughly one-ninth the chance of halving wealth before doubling, versus one-third for full Kelly); quarter-Kelly (κ = 0.25) retains approximately half of full-Kelly growth with much smaller drawdowns. The growth-versus-security argument is reinforced by the practical observation that Kelly sizing is acutely sensitive to estimation error in p̂: if p̂ is overestimated, full Kelly leverages aggressively into a bet whose true edge is smaller than believed, and the drawdown distribution becomes catastrophic. The team's baseline κ = 0.25 is therefore well-supported by both the formal growth-versus-security analysis and the practical estimation-error argument.

Carver (2015, *Systematic Trading*, Harriman House) is the principal practitioner reference for combining Kelly-style sizing with volatility targeting in a systematic-futures context. Carver (2015) argues that "using the full Kelly criteria is far too aggressive for most traders" and recommends a "25 per cent annualised volatility target" as the baseline for diversified-futures portfolios run with discretionary trader Sharpe expectations of approximately 1.0. Carver (2023, *Advanced Futures Trading Strategies*, Harriman House) updates this guidance for multi-strategy portfolios, retaining the volatility-targeting overlay while introducing strategy-level capital weighting based on per-strategy Sharpe estimates and inter-strategy correlations. [NOTE FOR WRITEUP LEAD: page-number verification for the two Carver quotes pending direct access to the print editions; the wording above is consistent with the team's working notes and with secondary citations in the systematic-futures literature.]

The volatility-targeting overlay itself — adjusting portfolio leverage to maintain a constant ex ante volatility target rather than a constant capital weight — has a substantial empirical literature. Moreira and Muir (2017, *Journal of Finance*) demonstrate that volatility-timed portfolios in US equities, the equity factor zoo, and currency carry deliver higher Sharpe ratios than constant-leverage benchmarks, with the Sharpe improvement attributable to the negative correlation between volatility and subsequent returns. Harvey et al. (2018, *Journal of Portfolio Management*) extend this to multi-asset futures and confirm a Sharpe-ratio uplift of approximately 0.2 from volatility targeting versus constant leverage. The mechanism is straightforward: volatility is persistent and partially predictable from realised volatility, so scaling leverage inversely to recent volatility avoids over-leveraging during turbulent regimes and under-leveraging during calm regimes. We adopt a Garman–Klass-based realised volatility estimate as the input to the volatility-targeting overlay, consistent with our choice of primary volatility estimator in Section A1.

The published 2023–2026 work on probability-conditioned position sizing is sparse but growing. The closest published work is Joubert (2022) on probability-aware Kelly sizing in meta-labelling pipelines, which reports that thresholding the Kelly fraction at a minimum confidence floor (no position taken when p̂ falls below some threshold, regardless of nominal Kelly recommendation) improves the realised Sharpe ratio relative to unconditional Kelly sizing. The team adopts a confidence floor of 0.55 in the spirit of this finding: trades with meta-model confidence below 0.55 receive zero size, regardless of the formal Kelly recommendation. Transaction-cost-aware Kelly literature is led by Davis and Norman (1990, *Mathematics of Operations Research*) on the classical no-trade region for fixed costs, and more recently by Garleanu and Pedersen (2013, *Journal of Finance*) on dynamic trading with quadratic costs; we incorporate transaction-cost-adjusted Kelly by simply scaling f* by 1 minus the round-trip cost in volatility units, a heuristic consistent with the Garleanu–Pedersen analytical solution in the small-cost limit.

## A1. Adjacent: OHLC-Based Volatility Estimation

The OHLC volatility estimation literature begins with Parkinson (1980, *Journal of Business*), who derived the high-low range estimator and demonstrated that it is approximately 5× more efficient than the close-to-close estimator at the same number of daily observations. In the same issue of the same journal, Garman and Klass (1980, *Journal of Business*) introduced a four-price estimator using open, high, low and close, with efficiency approximately 7.4× versus close-to-close. The efficiency ordering — close-to-close < Parkinson < Garman–Klass — is robust across the published literature and reflects the increased information content of using more of the intraday price path. Yang and Zhang (2000, *Journal of Business*) derived an estimator that combines overnight return information with intraday Garman–Klass terms in a way that is independent of drift, and demonstrated still higher efficiency under empirically realistic conditions; their estimator is approximately 14× efficient relative to close-to-close in their reference simulations. Meilijson (2011, *REVSTAT Statistical Journal*) revisited the Garman–Klass derivation, identified a subtle inefficiency in the original derivation, and proposed a slightly more efficient alternative that has gained limited adoption in practice.

The argument for preferring Garman–Klass over Parkinson on energy futures specifically rests on the overnight-gap behaviour induced by scheduled information releases. Parkinson's estimator implicitly assumes continuous trading with zero overnight drift; on days containing scheduled releases that produce overnight gaps, Parkinson systematically underestimates volatility because the gap is not captured by the intraday high-low range. Garman–Klass incorporates an open-close term that captures jump-like overnight behaviour and is consequently better calibrated on days with scheduled releases. The published empirical evidence directly comparing the two estimators on energy futures is thin, but Korkusuz, Kambouroudis and McMillan (2023, *International Review of Financial Analysis*) provide a comprehensive cross-asset comparison and find that Garman–Klass dominates Parkinson on energy commodities in forecasting realised variance one period ahead, though Yang–Zhang in turn dominates Garman–Klass on most series.

The Yang–Zhang estimator has been challenged on the basis of its sensitivity to microstructure noise and the difficulty of estimating its component covariances on noisy intraday data, but remains the efficiency frontier in the published literature for daily-frequency OHLC inputs. For our application, the team adopts Garman–Klass as the primary estimator on the grounds that it is materially more efficient than Parkinson with no meaningful additional implementation complexity, while remaining more robust than Yang–Zhang to microstructure noise at the daily-bar frequency. Parkinson is retained as a robustness check.

## A2. Adjacent: Deep Learning for Time Series (VSN/TFT)

The Temporal Fusion Transformer of Lim, Arık, Loeff and Pfister (2021, *International Journal of Forecasting*) is the dominant interpretable deep-learning architecture for multi-horizon forecasting with mixed static, time-varying-observed, and time-varying-known input. The TFT comprises three substantive sub-architectures: a Variable Selection Network (VSN) that produces per-feature selection weights via softmax over Gated Residual Network (GRN) outputs, an LSTM encoder-decoder for local temporal processing, and a multi-head attention layer for long-range temporal dependencies. For our application — tabular feature engineering at daily frequency with no native sequential structure beyond what we explicitly encode in lag features — only the VSN sub-architecture is relevant. The GRN block, as described by Lim et al. (2021), is a small feed-forward block with skip connections and gating layers: input embedding goes through a fully connected layer with ELU activation, a second fully connected layer, gated linear unit gating, and a residual connection back to the original input, normalised by layer norm. Per-feature selection weights are computed by passing each feature embedding through its own GRN, concatenating, and applying softmax to produce a probability distribution over features for each instance and time step. The feature embeddings are then summed weighted by these selection probabilities, producing a single context vector per time step. For standalone VSN training (without the downstream LSTM and attention layers), the loss function is whatever is appropriate to the prediction target — quantile loss in the original TFT, but cross-entropy for classification or MSE for regression — and the training procedure is conventional mini-batch stochastic gradient descent with Adam.

The application of VSN or TFT to financial data in the peer-reviewed literature is, as of 2026, still limited. The closest applications we identified are: Wu, Hu and Yang (2023, *Journal of Financial Data Science*) on TFT for daily equity-volatility forecasting, who report that TFT marginally beats LSTM and substantially beats ARFIMA baselines in out-of-sample MSE; and Wood, Roberts and Zohren (2022, *Journal of Financial Data Science*) on deep momentum networks for trend-following in futures, which use a related but distinct architecture and demonstrate Sharpe-ratio improvements over linear trend benchmarks on the MOP universe. We have not located a peer-reviewed VSN-versus-LightGBM horse-race on tabular financial data; this comparison appears to be open.

The architectural choice of VSN-only rather than full TFT is deliberate. The full TFT has on the order of 100,000–500,000 parameters in typical configurations, which — by the IKM small-data argument — is excessive for our daily-frequency multi-asset universe. The VSN sub-architecture alone has on the order of 10,000 parameters for typical feature counts, which is closer to the regularised-tree-ensemble parameter budget and more defensible given our sample size. The selection-weight interpretability of VSN is a secondary motivation: the softmax weights provide an automatic feature-importance signal that can be compared with SHAP attributions on the same trained model, supplying the comparison flagged in Section 5.

## A3. Adjacent: Time-Series Momentum in Futures (Deeper Cut)

Beyond the headline MOP (2012) finding summarised in Section 2, the deeper cut on time-series momentum in futures concerns its persistence post-2009 and its crisis-alpha character. The strongest published out-of-sample replication is Hurst, Ooi and Pedersen (2017, *Journal of Portfolio Management*), who extend the MOP factor over 137 years (1880–2016) across approximately 50 markets and four asset classes. They find that the time-series momentum premium is positive and statistically significant in every decade of the long history with the exception of the 2010s, where the premium has declined but remains positive. The declining-magnitude finding is consistent with the more skeptical assessment of Kim, Tse and Wald (2016, *Journal of Empirical Finance*), who argue that the bulk of MOP's reported Sharpe is attributable to the volatility-scaling weighting rather than to time-series momentum per se, and that unscaled time-series momentum is not statistically distinguishable from buy-and-hold for most asset classes individually.

The crisis-alpha character of time-series momentum — that trend-following in futures performs particularly well during equity drawdowns — is established in the peer-reviewed literature primarily through Hurst, Ooi and Pedersen (2017) and through Baltas and Kosowski (2020, *Journal of Financial and Quantitative Analysis*) on the crisis-alpha properties of multi-asset trend strategies. Hurst, Ooi and Pedersen document that time-series momentum delivered positive returns in 8 of the 10 largest equity drawdowns of the 20th and 21st centuries; the mechanism is that sustained equity drawdowns are typically accompanied by sustained trends in bonds (rallying as yields fall), currencies (flight-to-quality flows), and commodities (recession-driven oil declines), and the time-series momentum factor captures these correlated trends.

MOP (2012) themselves report cross-asset-class results indicating that time-series momentum is significant and economically meaningful in each of equities, currencies, commodities, and bonds, but with the largest Sharpe ratios in the currency and commodity groups. The asset-class ordering — currencies ≈ commodities > bonds > equities — is robust to the volatility-scaling correction in Kim, Tse and Wald (2016), though the magnitudes shrink uniformly. For the team's universe — Energy, Metals, Equity Index, Rates futures — the published literature thus predicts the strongest pure trend signals in Energy and Metals (commodities), with somewhat weaker signals in Rates (bonds) and Equity Index, calibrating the prior on which the meta-model's filtering decisions will be evaluated.
