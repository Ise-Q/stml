# LR-8 — Meta-Labelling on Weak Mean-Reversion Primary Signals: Framing the Honest Null

## TL;DR

- **A near-zero meta-labelling edge is the *predicted* outcome, not an anomaly.** Meta-labelling, as López de Prado defines it, is explicitly a *false-positive filter on an existing skilful side-signal* — "It is not its purpose to come up with a betting opportunity." With a primary that has ~0.52–0.60 hit-rates and near-zero information coefficients (IC), there is no skill to filter, so CPCV-AUC ≈ 0.5 and cluster-MDA ≈ 0 are exactly what the theory anticipates. There is **no peer-reviewed closed-form numeric floor** on primary quality; the precondition is a qualitative modelling premise plus empirical heuristics (Grinold's Fundamental Law supplies the only *algebraic* backbone: IC ≈ 0 ⇒ IR ≈ 0 regardless of breadth or sizing).
- **Attributing the positive barrier-exact Sharpe to MECHANISM, not TIMING, is well-supported.** The Henriksson–Merton (1981) and Treynor–Mazuy (1966) decompositions are the canonical tools, and the sign rule is unambiguous: positive-and-significant ⇒ timing skill; negative/insignificant ⇒ none. Negative HM z-scores (z < 0, p > 0.95) on Equity/Metals/All-11 sleeves therefore formally license the "mechanism-not-timing" reading, corroborated by the volatility-targeting (Harvey et al. 2018; Moreira & Muir 2017) and portfolio-construction (Ciliberti & Gualdi 2018) literatures, which document Sharpe gains arising from *structure* rather than directional forecasting.
- **The negative result can be presented as rigorous, not embarrassing.** Precedent (Cenesizoglu & Timmermann 2012; Leitch & Tanner 1991; the applied MQL5 meta-labelling series) shows that statistical accuracy (AUC/MSE) routinely decouples from economic value, and that the credible way to report a null is a like-for-like primary-vs-meta comparison under OOS/walk-forward/CPCV with conclusions decoupled from any single accuracy metric, wrapped in backtest-overfitting hygiene (Deflated Sharpe Ratio, PBO). **Caveat:** "Kang & Kim (2025)" could not be verified and should be dropped (see Caveats).

## Key Findings

1. **No proven numeric threshold exists** below which a secondary model "cannot" add value. The binding statement is qualitative: meta-labelling corrects *low precision* on a *high-recall* primary that already sets the side. This is an **ASSUMED modelling premise** (López de Prado 2018), not a theorem.
2. **The Fundamental Law of Active Management (Grinold 1989; Grinold & Kahn) is the one piece of PROVEN algebra** that bounds the result: IR = IC·√BR (generalised IR ≈ TC·IC·√BR). With IC ≈ 0, IR ≈ 0 *regardless* of breadth, transfer coefficient, or sizing — so a directional secondary model is structurally incapable of manufacturing an information ratio the primary does not possess. A near-zero-IC primary is, moreover, the empirical norm rather than an outlier: Zhang, Guo & Cao (Wells Fargo, 2020, "Information Coefficient as a Performance Measure of Stock Selection Models", arXiv:2010.08601) note that "a realistic stock selection model can hardly have an IC materially different from zero… the real IC distribution tends to be narrow, concentrating around zero," with even strong models "barely" above zero (IC ≈ 0.05–0.1).
3. **When the López de Prado precondition is violated, meta-labelling degrades to noise and can hurt.** A secondary classifier on a near-random primary is fitting noise; a *negative* Henriksson–Merton z is the formal signature of systematically perverse (worse-than-random) timing, consistent with an overlay that removes signal rather than noise.
4. **Mechanism-not-timing is the expected resolution.** Both the timing-vs-selectivity literature and the risk-overlay literature say that when directional skill is absent, residual Sharpe must come from exits, volatility targeting, sizing, regime conditioning or portfolio construction — non-directional structure.
5. **Mean-reversion specificity reinforces Q1.** Short-horizon MR signals are documented as having low, regime-dependent, unstable hit-rates whose ceiling bounds any secondary model's achievable AUC; the literature treats MR (high win-rate, small wins, left-tail damage) and momentum (positively skewed) asymmetrically.

## Details — Conditional Efficacy and the Violated Precondition (Q1–Q2)

### Q1 — Conditional efficacy: is there a floor on primary-signal quality? (no proven numeric floor)

The meta-labelling literature gives a **qualitative precondition, not a quantitative floor**. López de Prado (2018, *Advances in Financial Machine Learning*, Ch. 3, §3.6–3.7, pp. 50–51) frames the technique entirely as a precision-repair operation on an *existing* side-signal: "Meta-labeling is particularly helpful when you want to achieve higher F1-scores. First, we build a model that achieves high recall, even if the precision is not particularly high. Second, we correct for the low precision by applying meta-labeling to the positives predicted by the primary model." He is explicit that the secondary model is not a signal generator: "the role of the secondary ML algorithm is to determine whether a positive from the primary (exogenous) model is true or false. It is not its purpose to come up with a betting opportunity. Its purpose is to determine whether we should act or pass on the opportunity that has been presented." (Verbatim wording confirmed via the mlfinpy/mlfinlab documentation, which reproduces the book passage near-word-for-word; flagged as a *secondary* reproduction because the copyrighted book text could not be opened directly.)

Crucially, **no peer-reviewed source supplies a closed-form numeric threshold** (e.g., "hit-rate must exceed X" or "IC must exceed Y") below which a secondary act/skip model provably cannot add value. What exists is:

- **PROVEN algebra (the only formal bound):** Grinold's (1989) Fundamental Law of Active Management, IR = IC·√BR, generalised by Grinold & Kahn to IR ≈ TC·IC·√BR. As standard expositions state plainly, "If the IC is zero, the IR is also zero, regardless of the breadth of opportunities" — skill is a necessary condition for risk-adjusted excess return. This is the rigorous reason a near-zero-IC primary cannot be rescued by a secondary model that only re-weights or filters: re-sizing operates through breadth/transfer-coefficient terms, which multiply an IC that is ≈ 0.
- **ASSUMED modelling premise:** López de Prado's "high recall, trade some recall for precision" condition — a design heuristic, not a theorem with a bound.
- **EMPIRICAL/heuristic guidance:** practitioner statements that meta-labelling "is not applicable to all strategies" and "depends heavily on the accuracy of the primary model, emphasizing the need for a well-constructed initial model" (Wikipedia's *Meta-Labeling* article, citing Joubert 2022; flagged as tertiary but traceable to the JFDS source). Joubert (2022) himself deconstructs meta-labelling into components — information advantage, modelling for false positives, and bet sizing — none of which can extract directional information that is not present in the primary.

**Bottom line for §3/§5:** with per-instrument hit-rates clustered at ~0.52–0.60 and several ICs near zero (one undefined), the absence of secondary edge is the *predicted* consequence of operating below the qualitative precondition, and is *bounded* by the Fundamental Law's IC ≈ 0 ⇒ IR ≈ 0 algebra — not merely an observed disappointment.

### Q2 — The López de Prado framing when violated: noise, or active harm?

When the "primary has some skill" premise holds, the secondary model raises F1 by filtering false positives — "Meta-labeling will increase your F1-score by filtering out the false positives, where the majority of positives have already been identified by the primary model" (López de Prado 2018, p. 51). When the premise is violated, two failure modes follow:

- **Degradation to noise (the central case):** with no exploitable signal, the classifier fits sampling noise; out-of-sample this collapses to AUC ≈ 0.5 and feature-importance (MDA) ≈ 0 — precisely the project's empirical picture. The applied MQL5 "Meta-Labeling the Classics (Part 1)" study is candid about this regime, reporting a held-out classifier accuracy of **48% — "below chance"** — and conceding "the article's central claim is not that a meta-classifier can reliably identify winning RSI signals."
- **Active harm (the negative-HM signature):** a *negative* Henriksson–Merton z-statistic is the formal fingerprint of worse-than-random timing. In a meta-labelling context this arises when the act/skip overlay preferentially passes on the trades that would have worked and acts on those that fail — i.e., it removes signal rather than noise. The project's negative HM z (z < 0, p > 0.95) on Equity/Metals/All-11 sleeves is consistent with this: the overlay is not merely uninformative, it is mildly counter-productive as a *timing* device, which is exactly why P&L must be sourced from the non-directional mechanism instead.

This distinction matters for tone: the honest framing is "the secondary model adds no *directional/timing* skill (and is marginally adverse as a timer), exactly as predicted for a sub-precondition primary," not "the method is broken."

## Details — Timing-vs-Mechanism Decomposition and Negative-Result Precedent (Q3–Q4)

### Q3 — Sizing vs timing decomposition (Henriksson–Merton, Treynor–Mazuy, Pesaran–Timmermann)

The literature gives a clean, canonical machinery for separating *market-timing skill* from everything else (selectivity, structure, mechanism), and it supports attributing the observed Sharpe to mechanism.

- **Treynor & Mazuy (1966)**, "Can Mutual Funds Outguess the Market?", *Harvard Business Review* 44(4): 131–136 — add a **quadratic** term to the CAPM regression; a positive, significant coefficient on the squared market excess return (the "gamma") indicates convex, timing-consistent payoff. The canonical empirical verdict from the original study was negative: as the standard exposition records, "only one of the 57 funds has a significantly positive value of [the timing coefficient] at the 5% level of significance. Just by chance, one would expect that three funds would show significantly positive values… since 5% of 57 is 2.85" — i.e. fewer than chance would predict.
- **Henriksson & Merton (1981)**, "On Market Timing and Investment Performance. II. Statistical Procedures for Evaluating Forecasting Skills", *Journal of Business* 54(4): 513–533, DOI 10.1086/296144 — a **piecewise-linear / option-payoff** (dual-beta) formulation; the timing coefficient is positive and significant under genuine timing skill. The page range is **513–533** (verified via the University of Chicago Press / RePEc record and the *Journal of Business* table of contents; the "513–534" that appears in some aggregators, e.g. several SCIRP reference pages, is a transcription error).
- **Pesaran & Timmermann (1992)**, "A Simple Nonparametric Test of Predictive Performance", *Journal of Business & Economic Statistics* 10(4): 461–465, DOI 10.1080/07350015.1992.10509922 — a market-timing/directional-accuracy test on a contingency table; under the null that the forecast cannot predict direction, the standardised statistic is asymptotically N(0,1).

**Sign-interpretation rule (PROVEN, canonical):** for all three, a **positive and statistically significant** coefficient/statistic ⇒ evidence of timing/directional skill; a **negative or insignificant** value ⇒ no timing skill. A *negative* HM coefficient/z specifically indicates perverse (worse-than-random) timing. The project's HM result (z < 0, p > 0.95 for Equity/Metals/All-11; neutral for Energy) therefore formally **rejects timing skill** and is consistent with mildly adverse timing — directly licensing the mechanism-not-timing attribution.

**Known sign-convention / software pitfall (FLAG):** the Henriksson–Merton regressor admits two equivalent formulations (an "up-market" indicator vs. a max(0, –Rm) "down-market protection" term), and different statistical packages adopt different conventions, which **flips the sign** of the reported timing coefficient. R's `PerformanceAnalytics::MarketTiming` implements both TM and HM forms and is a documented site of this confusion. Before publishing a sign, confirm the regressor definition in the actual code path and verify against a hand-worked toy case; otherwise a genuinely negative timing signal can masquerade as positive (or vice versa). This caveat should appear in §5 alongside the HM numbers.

### Q4 — Negative-result precedent: how to frame a methodologically rigorous null

Published and applied work establishes that a meta-/secondary overlay failing to beat the primary OOS is a legitimate, reportable finding, and models the tone:

- **Statistical accuracy ≠ economic value (the core precedent).** Cenesizoglu & Timmermann (2012), "Do Return Prediction Models Add Economic Value?", *Journal of Banking & Finance* 36(11): 2974–2987, find it "very common for models to produce higher out-of-sample mean squared forecast errors than a model assuming a constant equity premium, yet simultaneously add economic value," and that the correlation between statistical and economic performance "tends to be weak and only explains **a small part** of the cross-sectional variation in different models' economic value" (their RMSE-over-economic-value predictive power is "generally less than 10%"). Quote the authors' phrase "a small part" rather than paraphrasing the <10% as a hard law. Leitch & Tanner (1991), "Economic Forecast Evaluation: Profits versus the Conventional Error Measures", *American Economic Review* 81(3): 580–590, established the converse direction — directional accuracy correlates with profit far better than MSE/MAE do. **Implication for §5:** an AUC ≈ 0.5 does not by itself condemn the strategy, and a positive Sharpe does not by itself prove directional skill; the two questions must be tested separately (hence HM/TM for timing, P&L attribution for mechanism). This is the methodological heart of decoupling the conclusion from any single accuracy metric.
- **Applied meta-labelling nulls.** The MQL5 "MetaTrader 5 Machine Learning Blueprint / Meta-Labeling the Classics" series (Patrick M. Njoroge, 2025–2026) provides like-for-like primary-vs-meta comparisons on EURUSD under OOS windows. Part 1 (RSI mean-reversion primary) is explicit that the value-add is *structural, not predictive*: "the drawdown finding … holds regardless of classifier accuracy, because the mechanism is structural rather than predictive," with the classifier itself at 48% (below chance). This is verified applied/grey literature and should be cited as such (not peer-reviewed). The Part 3 (trend-scanning) instalment reports Sharpe uplift for trend-scanning labels but is an applied blog post; **do not quote its specific uplift figures as established** — flagged for direct verification before any number is reproduced. The author has also publicly acknowledged a performance-metric calculation error in a related instalment, reinforcing the need for caution with the series' headline numbers.
- **Non-peer-reviewed thesis precedent (use with heavy caveat).** Wölner-Hanssen (2022), a Lund University bachelor thesis, "Evaluating the Effect of Meta-Labeling on … Equity Market Neutral Strategy," reports a *positive* meta-labelling effect — but its apparent "skill" gain depends on re-injecting primary-model features into the secondary model, which is effective information leakage and inflates the result. Cite only as an illustrative, caveated counter-example of how *not* to claim a positive result, never as evidence that meta-labelling adds directional skill.

**The credibility wrapper for a null.** Bailey & López de Prado (2014), "The Deflated Sharpe Ratio", *Journal of Portfolio Management* 40(5): 94–107, and Bailey, Borwein, López de Prado & Zhu (2017), "The Probability of Backtest Overfitting", *Journal of Computational Finance* — together with Combinatorial Purged Cross-Validation (López de Prado 2018) — let the team frame the null as *robust*: the DSR corrects an apparently strong Sharpe for selection bias and non-normality, and PBO quantifies the chance that an in-sample "winner" is overfit. Reporting that the *primary's* mechanism-driven Sharpe survives deflation while the *secondary's* directional contribution does not is the strongest possible honest-negative framing. (Deflation statistics are owned by LR-6; here they are invoked only as the hygiene frame for the null.)

## Details — Mechanism Channels and Mean-Reversion Specificity (Q5–Q6)

### Q5 — What would have helped: value-add from structure, not classification

The literature is consistent that when directional skill is near-random, residual risk-adjusted performance must come from **non-directional structure** — risk/volatility overlays, position sizing, regime conditioning, and portfolio construction — not from a better act/skip classifier. This is precisely the "mechanism, not skill" resolution.

- **Volatility targeting.** Harvey, Hoyle, Korgaonkar, Rattray, Sargaison & van Hemert (2018), "The Impact of Volatility Targeting", *Journal of Portfolio Management* 45(1): 14–33, show that volatility scaling raises Sharpe ratios and "reduces the likelihood of extreme returns, across all asset classes" — a benefit obtained with *no directional forecast at all*. Critically, they document **asset-class heterogeneity**: the Sharpe gain "only holds for 'risk assets', such as equity and credit," linked to the leverage effect, while "for bonds, currencies, and commodities the impact of volatility targeting on the Sharpe ratio is negligible." This directly informs the project's sleeve heterogeneity (e.g., why a vol-targeting mechanism may lift the Equity sleeve's Sharpe but do little for Metals/commodities), and it cleanly separates the tail-risk benefit (universal) from the Sharpe benefit (concentrated in risk assets).
- **Volatility-managed portfolios.** Moreira & Muir (2017), "Volatility-Managed Portfolios", *Journal of Finance* 72(4): 1611–1644, DOI 10.1111/jofi.12513, find that scaling exposure down when volatility is high "produce[s] large alphas, increase[s] Sharpe ratios" because "changes in volatility are not offset by proportional changes in expected returns" — again, a *mechanism* result, independent of directional timing. **Balance/caveat:** Cederburg, O'Doherty, Wang & Yan (2020), "On the Performance of Volatility-Managed Portfolios", *Journal of Financial Economics* 138(1): 95–117, find these gains are largely "not implementable in real time": volatility-managed versions outperformed in only 53 of 103 cases, only eight strategies yielded consistently higher Sharpe ratios (concentrated in momentum/profitability/betting-against-beta), and the real-time combination earned a lower certainty-equivalent return than the original portfolio in 72 of 103 cases (their OOS managed-market Sharpe was 0.42 vs 0.46 unmanaged). So claim the *mechanism channel* exists, but do not overstate its universality or net-of-costs magnitude.
- **Portfolio construction.** Ciliberti & Gualdi (2018), "Portfolio Construction Matters" (arXiv:1810.08384; *Journal of Portfolio Management* 2020, 46(7): 46–57), show that "an optimized portfolio construction algorithm allows one to significantly improve the Sharpe Ratio, reduce sector exposures and volatility fluctuations" — Sharpe improvement from *construction, not signal*, holding the underlying factor fixed. This is the cleanest cite for "the same raw signal yields very different Sharpe depending on structural choices."

Taken together, these establish that the project's attribution — positive P&L from triple-barrier/barrier-exact exits plus volatility targeting rather than directional timing — is the *expected* resolution for a near-random primary, not a post-hoc rationalisation. The four-way convergence (AUC ≈ 0.5, MDA ≈ 0, negative HM z, and P&L surviving only through the exit/sizing mechanism) is internally consistent with this body of theory.

### Q6 — Mean-reversion specificity: does an MR primary bound the secondary AUC?

There is a defensible MR-specific version of Q1. Short-horizon mean-reversion signals are characterised in the literature as having **low, regime-dependent, and unstable hit-rates**, and MR is treated asymmetrically from momentum:

- **Horizon- and regime-dependence.** Practitioner and academic sources describe mean reversion as "a horizon-specific bet," not a universal property, and short-horizon reversal edges as fragile and decaying ("yesterday's close→close return helps forecast tomorrow's … but this strategy over recent times has tended to lose its edge"). The reversal premise (extremes revert) holds in ranging regimes and *fails systematically in trending regimes*, which is exactly the failure mode the MQL5 RSI case study dramatises (the 2022 EURUSD trend producing compounding losses from a mechanically firing MR rule).
- **Payoff-shape asymmetry.** The literature contrasts mean reversion (higher win-rate, smaller normal wins, occasional left-tail damage — i.e. negatively skewed) with momentum/trend (positively skewed, many small losses, occasional large wins). A high nominal win-rate with negative skew is *exactly* the configuration in which raw hit-rate overstates exploitable directional information, and in which a secondary classifier has little stable structure to learn.
- **Implication for achievable AUC.** Because the primary's directional information is both low and regime-unstable, the *ceiling* on a secondary act/skip model's out-of-sample AUC is correspondingly low: the meta-model can only separate winners from losers to the extent the primary's success is conditionally predictable, and for short-horizon MR that conditional predictability is thin and non-stationary. This is consistent with the observed CPCV-AUC ≈ 0.5. **Honest scoping note:** this MR-vs-momentum asymmetry is well-supported as an *empirical/heuristic* regularity, but I found **no peer-reviewed paper that states a formal, MR-specific bound on meta-model AUC** — so frame it as a strong, literature-consistent expectation, not a proven theorem. The general bound remains the Fundamental Law (IC ≈ 0 ⇒ IR ≈ 0).

## Recommendations

**Staged, concrete next steps for §3 (model development) and §5 (evaluation):**

1. **State the null as predicted, with a three-tier evidentiary spine.** In §3, open the meta-model subsection by citing López de Prado (2018, pp. 50–51) for the *premise* (meta-labelling filters false positives from a high-recall side-signal; "It is not its purpose to come up with a betting opportunity"), then state that this premise is *violated* by the provided primary (~0.55 hit-rate, IC ≈ 0). In §5, present the bound: Grinold (1989) — IC ≈ 0 ⇒ IR ≈ 0 — as the *proven algebra* explaining why CPCV-AUC ≈ 0.5 and MDA ≈ 0 are expected, not disappointing. Explicitly label each claim PROVEN (Fundamental Law algebra; HM/TM sign rule), ASSUMED PREMISE (López de Prado's exploitable-skill precondition), or EMPIRICAL/HEURISTIC ("needs base alpha"/win-rate intuitions; MR-AUC ceiling).
2. **Make the timing-vs-mechanism decomposition the centrepiece of the attribution.** Report the Henriksson–Merton (1981) z per sleeve with the explicit sign rule, and add a Treynor–Mazuy (1966) gamma and a Pesaran–Timmermann (1992) statistic as corroboration. State that negative/insignificant timing coefficients *formally reject* timing skill, and that the surviving Sharpe is therefore attributable to the exit mechanism + volatility targeting (Harvey et al. 2018; Moreira & Muir 2017) and structure (Ciliberti & Gualdi 2018). **Before publishing any HM sign, audit the regressor convention** (see Q3 pitfall) and verify against a toy case.
3. **Present the null with full overfitting hygiene.** Show the like-for-like primary-vs-meta comparison under CPCV/walk-forward; report that the primary's mechanism-driven Sharpe survives Deflated-Sharpe (Bailey & López de Prado 2014) and low PBO (Bailey et al. 2017), while the secondary adds no deflation-surviving directional contribution. Cite Cenesizoglu & Timmermann (2012) and Leitch & Tanner (1991) to pre-empt the "but AUC is only 0.5" objection — the AUC≠P&L disconnect is a documented regularity, not a flaw in the work.
4. **Qualify the volatility-targeting claim by asset class.** Use Harvey et al. (2018) and Cederburg et al. (2020) to state that the Sharpe benefit concentrates in equity/credit and is weak/negligible (and often not real-time-implementable) for commodities/FX/bonds — this both explains the Energy/Metals sleeve heterogeneity and inoculates against over-claiming.

**Thresholds that would change these recommendations:** (a) if a re-specified or alternative primary achieved a *stable* OOS IC materially above ~0.03–0.05 (per Zhang et al. 2020, already a strong model) with hit-rates consistently >0.55 across instruments, the López de Prado precondition would be met and a positive meta-labelling edge would become plausible — re-run the AUC/MDA/HM battery; (b) if the HM/TM/PT coefficients turned positive-and-significant after the sign-convention audit, the timing-skill rejection would no longer hold and the mechanism-only attribution would need revisiting; (c) if the meta-overlay's Sharpe contribution survived DSR/PBO, it could be promoted from "no edge" to "marginal edge."

## Caveats

- **"Kang & Kim (2025)" is UNVERIFIED — do not cite.** Targeted verification found no genuine 2025 Kang & Kim meta-labelling/ML-evaluation paper. The only real, related work is **Fu, Kang, Hong & Kim (2024)**, "Enhanced Genetic-Algorithm-Driven Triple Barrier Labeling Method and Machine Learning Approach for Pair Trading Strategy in Cryptocurrency Markets", *Mathematics* 12(5): 780, DOI 10.3390/math12050780 (four authors, 2024, triple-barrier/GA for pair trading — not a meta-labelling-null paper). "Kang & Kim (2025)" appears to be a misattribution/conflation of this paper. Either cite the correct 2024 four-author reference or drop it.
- **The López de Prado precondition quote is from a verbatim *secondary* reproduction** (mlfinpy/mlfinlab docs), not the copyrighted book PDF, which could not be opened directly. The wording matches across multiple independent reproductions (including López de Prado's own SSRN "10 Reasons Most ML Funds Fail"), and the §3.6 / pp. 50–51 location is consistent across sources, but the team should confirm against a physical/library copy before printing the page number as definitive.
- **"Primary must have skill" is an inference, not a single verbatim sentence.** López de Prado does not write a one-line numbered precondition; the requirement is unambiguous from the surrounding text (the secondary decides *size/whether-to-act*, not *side*, and operates on "the positives predicted by the primary model"). Frame it as a well-supported reading of an ASSUMED modelling premise, not as a quoted theorem.
- **No closed-form numeric floor exists** on primary hit-rate/IC/Sharpe in the peer-reviewed literature. The only formal bound is the Fundamental Law's IC ≈ 0 ⇒ IR ≈ 0; "win-rate > 50%" and "needs base alpha" are EMPIRICAL/heuristic, not proven thresholds. Do not present any numeric floor as established theory.
- **The MR-specific AUC ceiling is heuristic.** The MR-vs-momentum asymmetry (low, unstable, regime-dependent MR hit-rates; negative skew) is well-documented empirically, but no peer-reviewed source states a formal MR-specific bound on meta-model AUC. Present as a strong literature-consistent expectation.
- **Applied/grey-literature flags.** The MQL5 series (Njoroge) is applied/non-peer-reviewed; Part 1 (RSI) is verified, but Part 3 (trend-scanning) headline uplift figures are **not** verified and should not be quoted as numbers; the author has acknowledged a metric-calculation error in a related instalment. The Wölner-Hanssen (2022) Lund thesis is non-peer-reviewed and its positive result is leakage-contaminated — use only as a caveated "how not to" example.
- **Volatility-targeting realism.** Moreira & Muir (2017)'s alphas are partly spanning-regression artefacts not implementable in real time (Cederburg et al. 2020); cite the mechanism channel but qualify net-of-cost, real-time magnitude and asset-class scope.
