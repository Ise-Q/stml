## Details and Evidence

**Item 1 — Ang & Bekaert (2002).** Direct reading of the full published RFS article establishes that the equity universe is exactly three markets — US, UK, Germany — using MSCI total-return data, Jan 1970–Dec 1997 (Appendix A, p. 1182). Japan is named only in citation/justification contexts, never as data. Regime-conditional correlations: **Table 1 (p. 1148)**, US–UK bivariate, ρ₁ (bear) ≈ 0.6097 / ρ₂ (normal) ≈ 0.4455 (Wald test of ρ₁=ρ₂: p = 0.1556 — *not* significant); **Table 2 (p. 1149)**, US–UK–Germany trivariate, US–UK 0.5994/0.4056, US–Germany 0.4540/0.2620, UK–Germany 0.4523/0.4261. Crucial labelling point: in this paper **"regime 1" is the high-volatility bear regime** (p. 1148), the reverse of the intuitive ordering. The authors stress that "the evidence on higher volatility is much stronger than the evidence on higher correlation." The 0.67/0.48 and 0.39/0.27 quartet traces to **St. Louis Fed WP 2005-034** (Guidolin & Timmermann, *International Asset Allocation under Regime Switching, Skew and Kurtosis Preferences*), whose text reads: "Pairwise correlations between US stock returns and returns in Japan, Pacific ex-Japan, UK and Europe in the bear (bull) states are 0.39 (0.27), 0.65 (0.47), 0.67 (0.48) and 0.59 (0.45)." That paper itself notes it "extends Ang and Bekaert's [analysis] to include a wider set of stock markets." The review's numbers are correct *as quoted from the Fed paper* but were attributed to the wrong source.

**Item 2 — Carver (2015).** CXO Advisory's chapter-by-chapter notes quote verbatim: "From Chapter 9, 'Volatility Targeting': 'Deciding your overall trading risk is the most important decision you will have to make…'" confirming the chapter. The @ReformedTrader structured summary tags both the 25%-target sentence (tweet 66) and the full-Kelly quote (tweet 65) to **p. 146**. Carver's stated rule is that the percentage volatility target should equal the system's expected Sharpe ratio; he personally runs 25% and caps his own SR≈1.0 futures system at 37%. Both the volatility-target convention and the Kelly passage are therefore confirmable to **Chapter 9, p. 146 (high confidence)**.

**Item 3 — MacLean, Ziemba & Blazenko (1992).** The published article (*Management Science* 38, pp. 1562–1585) is paywalled, but its central Blackjack illustration (2% edge, p=0.51) is reproduced verbatim by the authors — including as **Fig. 1.1 in Ziemba & MacLean, "Using the Kelly Criterion for Investing," Ch. 1 of *Stochastic Optimization Methods in Finance and Energy* (Springer 2011), p. 5** — and in MacLean–Thorp–Ziemba's "Good and bad properties of the Kelly criterion." That table gives relative growth by Kelly fraction: κ=0.5 → 0.75, κ=1.0 → 1.00, with P[double before halve] of 0.89 and 0.67 respectively — i.e. half-Kelly halves-before-doubling ≈**1/9** of the time versus ≈**1/3** for full Kelly, exactly matching the review. At κ=0.25 relative growth interpolates to ≈0.43–0.44, so "approximately half" is a reasonable (slightly generous) rounding, while **"56%" cannot be sourced** to the paper — the review's decision to flag and remove it is correct.
