# Meta-Labelling: Conditions, Failure Regimes and the AUC≠P&L Disconnect — A Net-New Literature Review (Module T3.03)

## TL;DR

- **Meta-labelling adds classification edge only when three conditions hold simultaneously:** (i) the primary signal has high recall but poor precision over a non-trivial base rate of true positives, (ii) the secondary model exploits *incremental* information the primary did not (non-linear "information advantage" or regime/market-state features), and (iii) its output probabilities are well-calibrated so the score→bet-size map is sound [1][2][6][9]. Empirically the uplift is real but thin — peer-reviewed triple-barrier ML classifiers reach only ~0.6 OOS AUC versus a 0.5 baseline [12].
- **It predictably collapses toward OOS AUC ≈ 0.5 when** the primary is near-random (no exploitable conditional structure for the secondary to learn) [8], when train→OOS non-stationarity/regime shift invalidates the learned true/false boundary [9][13], or when apparent in-sample edge was a label-leakage/concurrency artefact that does not survive a clean temporal split [13][15].
- **AUC ≠ P&L:** the forecast-evaluation literature shows statistical accuracy is only weakly linked to economic value (RMSE explains <10% of cross-sectional economic value; profit tracks *directional* accuracy, not error measures) [3][4][10]. The literature recommends utility-aware tests (out-of-sample Sharpe, certainty-equivalent utility, Henriksson–Merton market timing) and calibration diagnostics (Brier, ECE/MCE) instead of AUC alone [2][5][11].

## (1) When meta-labelling adds classification edge over the primary

The enabling conditions are consistent across López de Prado's original framing and the subsequent peer-reviewed Joubert/Hudson & Thames line:

- **High-recall, low-precision primary with a non-trivial true-positive base rate.** López de Prado's design logic is to "first build a model that achieves high recall, even if the precision is not particularly high," then "correct for the low precision by applying meta-labeling to the positives predicted by the primary model," raising F1 by filtering false positives [already cited: López de Prado 2018; restated in 1]. The secondary model's *only* job is to decide "whether a positive from the primary (exogenous) model is true or false" — not to find new opportunities [1]. Edge therefore exists only where the primary's positives contain a separable true/false mixture.

- **An "information advantage": the secondary model must exploit structure the primary did not.** The Joubert/Meyer architecture papers formalise two channels — *information advantage* (e.g., a non-linear secondary model over a linear/discretionary primary) and *modelling for false positives* (adding regime/market-state features absent from the primary) [6][7]. If the secondary uses the *same* model class and feature set as the primary, it cannot add edge; the practitioner consensus is that meta-labelling "has the most benefit when the first-stage/primary model is simple (a linear model or a discretionary strategy)" [practitioner: QuantConnect].

- **Ensembles help specifically in multi-regime, non-linear data.** Thumm, Barucca & Joubert (2023) show ensembles "are especially beneficial when the underlying data consist of multiple regimes and are nonlinear in nature," and that ensembles "increase model efficiency by decreasing the rate of false positives, thus increasing the meta premium" [9].

- **Empirically, the uplift is real but modest.** Singh & Joubert's S&P 500 E-mini replication (2011–2019, trend-following and mean-reversion primaries + Random Forest secondary, triple-barrier labels) reports OOS precision rising from 0.48→0.54 and accuracy 48%→55% on one strategy [practitioner white paper: 8]. The coursework's existing Joubert (2022) figures (F1 0.42→0.57; Sharpe 0.6→1.1; 30–40% positive-class share) [already cited] sit at the optimistic end. (An earlier draft cited a peer-reviewed Korean-markets anchor, "Kang & Kim (2025)", for an OOS AUC ≈ 0.625 — **that reference was verified non-existent (no arXiv:2504.02249 meta-labelling paper) and is removed; it was a conflation of Fu, Kang, Hong & Kim (2024), a GA / triple-barrier *pairs-trading* study, not a meta-labelling-efficacy paper.**) The signal rests on the practitioner replications: even well-built secondary/triple-barrier models sit only modestly above the 0.5 random line — the "edge" is thin and fragile.

**Verdict:** the working hypothesis is **supported**. Meta-labelling adds classification edge when (i) the primary has decent recall but poor precision, (ii) a meaningful base rate of true positives exists, and (iii) the secondary model brings genuinely incremental (non-linear or regime) information. Absent any of these, there is nothing for the secondary model to learn.

## (2) The Predictable-Failure Regime: What Drives OOS AUC Toward 0.5

The predictable-failure regime is the mirror image of the enabling conditions. Three drivers push OOS AUC toward 0.5:

**(a) A near-random primary with no exploitable conditional structure.** If whether a primary signal is a true positive is not conditionally predictable from available features, the secondary model has nothing to separate and collapses to the no-skill diagonal (AUC = 0.5 is, by construction, the uninformative ROC where lowering the threshold raises true- and false-positive rates equally) [BIS EWI exposition]. López de Prado's own caveat is that meta-labelling presupposes a primary with predictive power; the replication literature is blunt: "as much as we show that meta-labeling works, it also needs a good primary algorithm… If the algorithm is bad then meta-labeling would likely only reduce the downside" [8]. The practitioner consensus reinforces this: "If primary model win rate is too low (<50%), meta model can only do so much… Without base alpha, meta-labeling cannot create it" [practitioner: waylandz]. A QuantConnect grid-search experiment found that "the meta-model is not able to squeeze additional returns compared with a single end-to-end model (average Sharpe ratio is lower)" [practitioner: QuantConnect] — the clearest "no added edge" statement available, though framed in Sharpe terms and not peer-reviewed.

**(b) Severe train→OOS non-stationarity / regime shift.** Triple-barrier labelling is "highly sensitive to parameter choices… which can lead to overfitting if not carefully tuned," and in non-stationary markets suffers "barrier misalignment," producing labels that no longer match the OOS regime [13]. Financial series are "non-stationary, autocorrelated and nonlinear" [9], so a secondary model fitted to one regime's true/false boundary will generalise poorly when the regime turns — exactly the mechanism that drives OOS AUC toward chance. The Joubert line treated this seriously enough to title an early talk "Meta-Labeling: Solving for Non-Stationarity" [practitioner: Joubert/LinkedIn].

**(c) Label leakage / concurrency artefacts as the *only* source of in-sample edge.** Triple-barrier labels are path-dependent and overlapping; concurrent labels "violate the IID assumptions of most ML algorithms… resulting in inflated in-sample performance followed by degradation in live trading" [13]. Where apparent in-sample edge is an artefact of overlapping labels, look-ahead in feature construction, or up-sampling leakage, it does not survive a clean temporal split — the OOS AUC reverts to 0.5. López de Prado's broader "10 reasons ML funds fail" catalogues these leakage and backtest-overfitting pathologies [15]; this connects to the coursework's already-cited Bailey & López de Prado (2014) and Harvey/Liu/Zhu (2016) on backtest overfitting and multiple testing.

**The asset-class angle (Equity-works / Energy-fails).** There *is* published support for the claim that the same secondary-model machinery can add edge on equity-index primaries but fail on energy-futures primaries — driven by asset-class differences in primary-signal quality and non-stationarity rather than anything specific to meta-labelling. Rad, Low, Miffre & Faff (2023), 'The Commodity Risk Premium and Neural Networks' (*Journal of Empirical Finance* 74), find their LSTM-DNN architecture "best captures the risk premium, which underscores the need to estimate models that are both nonlinear and recurrent" [16] — i.e., commodity risk premia demand the most complex models and remain hard to capture; a 2024 cross-country equity ML study characterises commodity-futures ML R² as "close to zero or negative" [17]. Wang & Zhang (2024) study 22 commodities (including energy) and report that "out-of-sample prediction errors for the majority of futures contracts are lowered compared with those obtained by the baseline models of AR(1) and forecast combinations," yet complex ML (trees, neural nets) *underperforms* simple LASSO and no common predictor set holds across commodities — evidence of heterogeneity and instability [18]. Energy specifically is the canonical regime-switching case: crude-oil markets exhibit Markov-switching regime shifts "not adequately explained by static indicators," epitomised by the NYMEX WTI May-2020 contract falling from $17.73 to settle at **–$37.63 per barrel on 20 April 2020** (intraday low –$40.32), the first sub-zero WTI price since trading began in 1983 (CFTC Interim Staff Report, Nov 2020; EIA) [19]. The counterweight is Moskowitz, Ooi & Pedersen (2012, **already cited**), who document significant time-series momentum across "equity index, currency, commodity, and bond futures" — so energy is not *un*-predictable, but its ML/signal predictability is weaker and less stable than equity indices.

**Net verdict on the split:** an Equity-works/Energy-fails split is **consistent with the published pattern** (weaker, more regime-dependent primary-signal quality in energy futures), though no single paper tests meta-labelling head-to-head across the two asset classes — so the inference is by synthesis, at medium confidence.

## (3) The AUC ≠ P&L Disconnect and What to Measure Instead

The core of the failure-regime argument is that classification metrics and economic value are only loosely coupled, so a meta-model can post a respectable AUC and still lose money — or vice versa. The evidence is long-standing and robust:

**High AUC/accuracy need not imply positive risk-adjusted P&L.** Leitch & Tanner (1991), 'Economic Forecast Evaluation: Profits versus the Conventional Error Measures' (*American Economic Review* 81(3)), is the seminal result: conventional error measures (MSE, RMSE) are poorly correlated with profits, whereas *directional accuracy* tracks profit far more closely — results show "profit has a stronger and more significant relationship with directional accuracy than accuracy as measured by conventional forecast evaluation statistics" [3]. Cenesizoglu & Timmermann (2012) extend this to return-prediction models: it is "very common for models to produce higher out-of-sample mean squared forecast errors than a [constant-premium benchmark], yet simultaneously add economic value," and the correlation between statistical and economic performance "tends to be weak," with RMSE explaining "generally less than 10%" of cross-sectional variation in economic value [4]. The decision-theoretic foundation is Granger & Pesaran (2000): statistical loss functions "rarely make sense in a specific policy context," and forecasts should be evaluated against the decision-maker's loss; they link the meteorological Kuipers score to finance's market-timing tests [10]. The implication for meta-labelling: optimising the secondary model for AUC/F1 can be orthogonal to — or even at odds with — Sharpe.

**Calibration matters more than discrimination for bet sizing.** Meta-labelling's economic value flows through *position sizing*: the secondary model's probability is mapped to a bet size (and, under Kelly, to leverage). For this map to be optimal the probabilities must be *calibrated*, not merely *discriminating*. AUC is invariant to any monotone re-scaling of probabilities, so a model can have excellent AUC yet badly miscalibrated probabilities that destroy Kelly P&L. Meyer, Barziy & Joubert (2023) test calibrated vs uncalibrated meta-model probabilities across six position-sizing algorithms and find "the performance of fixed position sizing methods is significantly improved by calibration," while data-driven sizers gain less [2]. The mechanism: Kelly is most aggressive in exactly the 0.52–0.65 probability band "where probability estimation errors are largest," so overconfident (miscalibrated) probabilities cause systematic over-betting and geometric-growth destruction — hence the recommendation for *fractional* Kelly and continuous re-calibration after regime shifts [practitioner: MQL5 calibration series]. Calibration is monitored with the Brier score (overall usefulness), Expected Calibration Error (average gap) and Maximum Calibration Error (worst-case region); a model with good ECE but poor MCE has a localised overconfidence zone that "averaged metrics will not reveal" [practitioner: MQL5]. This connects directly to the coursework's already-cited Kelly/utility line (MacLean/Ziemba/Blazenko 1992) and López de Prado's own Chapter-10 bet-sizing framework.

**What the literature recommends instead of AUC.** The consensus is *utility-aware* and *calibration-aware* evaluation:
1. **Economic loss functions / out-of-sample Sharpe and certainty-equivalent utility**, per Granger & Pesaran (2000) and Cenesizoglu & Timmermann (2012) [10][4].
2. **Directional-accuracy and market-timing tests** that are explicitly utility-linked: Pesaran & Timmermann (1992) and the Henriksson–Merton (1981) non-parametric market-timing test, which evaluates whether the conditional probability of a correct call exceeds chance in a 2×2 contingency framework — a utility-aware alternative to AUC [3][11][20].
3. **Calibration diagnostics** (Brier, ECE/MCE, reliability diagrams; Platt scaling / isotonic regression to repair miscalibration) *before* converting scores to bet sizes [2].

**Does meta-labelling's AUC and Sharpe diverge in practice?** The Joubert line reports gains in *both* (the already-cited F1 0.42→0.57 alongside Sharpe 0.6→1.1) — but the JFDS architecture papers deliberately report *strategy-level* metrics (Sharpe, max drawdown, the "meta premium") rather than AUC, precisely because the secondary model's value is sizing/filtering, not discrimination per se [6][9]. The QuantConnect counter-example — wider performance dispersion but *lower* average Sharpe despite a working classifier — is the canonical divergence case [practitioner]. The honest reading: a rising AUC is *necessary but not sufficient*; without calibration and a utility check it is not evidence of economic edge.

**Deliverable-framing paragraph (for coursework §3 and §5).** The expected-failure regime can be stated as follows: meta-labelling adds classification edge when the primary signal has high recall but poor precision over a non-trivial base rate of true positives, when the secondary model exploits incremental non-linear or regime information the primary did not, and when its probabilities are well-calibrated for bet sizing [1][2][6][9]; it predictably degrades toward OOS AUC ≈ 0.5 when the primary is near-random and carries no exploitable conditional structure [8][BIS], when train→OOS non-stationarity or regime shift invalidates the learned true/false boundary [9][13], or when apparent in-sample edge was an artefact of overlapping-label concurrency or look-ahead leakage that does not survive a clean temporal split [13][15]. Because statistical discrimination is only weakly linked to economic value [3][4][10], the failure should be diagnosed with utility-aware tests (out-of-sample Sharpe, certainty-equivalent utility, Henriksson–Merton market timing) and calibration diagnostics (Brier, ECE/MCE), not AUC alone [2][11][20]. On the specific Equity-works / Energy-fails hypothesis, the published pattern is **consistent**: machine-learning predictability of energy and broader commodity futures is documented as weak and unstable — neural-network architectures struggle to capture the commodity risk premium and require the most complex nonlinear-recurrent models [16], with commodity-futures ML R² characterised as "close to zero or negative" [17] and complex ML underperforming simple LASSO on commodities [18] — while crude-oil markets exhibit pronounced regime-switching non-stationarity [19], whereas equity-index primaries enjoy comparatively stronger and steadier conditional structure. The corollary is that a meta-model that adds edge on an equity-index primary may collapse to AUC ≈ 0.5 on an energy-futures primary not because the secondary architecture differs but because the *energy primary supplies no stable, separable true/false structure for it to learn* — exactly the failure regime above. This inference is by synthesis (medium confidence): no single study tests meta-labelling head-to-head on equity vs energy primaries, and time-series momentum is itself significant in commodity futures [already cited: Moskowitz, Ooi & Pedersen 2012], so the gap is one of degree and stability, not total absence of predictability.

## Recommendations

1. **Adopt a staged acceptance test for the meta-model.** Before claiming edge, require: (i) primary OOS recall demonstrably high with precision below it (otherwise there is no false-positive surplus to harvest); (ii) the secondary model uses a *different* model class and/or features (regime/market-state) from the primary [6][9]; (iii) a strict temporal (purged, embargoed) train/test split to kill concurrency leakage [13][15]. **Threshold that changes the decision:** if meta-model OOS AUC is within sampling error of 0.5, or not materially above the primary's own positives base rate, abandon the secondary layer — it is fitting noise.
2. **Evaluate on utility, not AUC.** Report out-of-sample Sharpe, certainty-equivalent utility and a Henriksson–Merton/Pesaran–Timmermann market-timing test alongside AUC/F1 [4][10][11]. Treat a rising AUC as necessary-but-not-sufficient; require a *simultaneous* Sharpe improvement net of costs before deployment.
3. **Calibrate before sizing.** Fit Platt/isotonic calibration on a held-out fold, monitor Brier, ECE and MCE, and size with *fractional* Kelly given the 0.52–0.65 danger band [2]. **Threshold:** if MCE flags a high-probability overconfidence region, cap bet size in that region regardless of the raw score.
4. **For the coursework's Equity vs Energy comparison, pre-register the expected asymmetry.** Expect meta-labelling to add edge on the equity-index primary and to drift toward AUC ≈ 0.5 on the energy-futures primary, attributing the difference to weaker, more regime-dependent energy primary-signal quality [16][17][18][19] — and test this by reporting per-asset primary recall/precision and a regime-stability diagnostic (e.g., rolling AUC) rather than a single pooled number. **Benchmark that would revise this:** if the energy primary itself shows stable OOS precision and a healthy true-positive base rate, the "Energy-fails" prediction should be withdrawn.
5. **Source-quality discipline.** Cite the JFDS architecture/calibration/ensemble trilogy [2][6][9] as primary (the former [12] Kang & Kim anchor is removed — verified non-existent); use Hudson & Thames / QuantConnect / MQL5 only as practitioner illustrations, never as primary empirical evidence.

## Caveats

- **No peer-reviewed "AUC ≈ 0.5 / no edge" result was located.** The explicit "secondary model adds nothing" claims are Sharpe-framed and from practitioner sources (QuantConnect, Hudson & Thames); the cleanest peer-reviewed number is a *modest* edge (AUC ≈ 0.625), not a documented collapse [8][12][practitioner]. The 0.5-collapse argument is therefore theoretically and definitionally grounded rather than directly demonstrated in a journal.
- **The JFDS meta-labelling papers report strategy-level metrics (Sharpe, MDD, "meta premium") more than AUC tables;** exact OOS AUC figures for the secondary model would require full-text access and were not extractable from abstracts/landing pages [6][7][9].
- **The Equity-works/Energy-fails verdict is by synthesis, not head-to-head testing.** It rests on separate commodity-vs-equity ML predictability studies [16][17][18] and energy regime-switching evidence [19]; time-series momentum *is* significant in energy futures [already cited: Moskowitz, Ooi & Pedersen 2012], so the asymmetry is one of degree/stability, not absolute predictability.
